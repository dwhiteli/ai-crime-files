Case #06: The Invisible Redline

How Facebook's ad targeting recreated illegal housing discrimination — automatically

Listen time: 35 minutes


---

[A Brief History of the Red Pen]

In the 1930s, the federal Home Owners' Loan Corporation created maps of American cities. Neighborhoods were color-coded by risk. Green meant safe for investment. Red meant too risky.

The red neighborhoods were almost always Black neighborhoods.

Banks used these maps to deny mortgages. Insurance companies used them to deny coverage. Entire communities were cut off from the wealth-building engine of homeownership. The practice became known as redlining.

The Fair Housing Act of 1968 made it illegal. It was one of the landmark achievements of the Civil Rights Movement.

Fifty years later, Facebook rebuilt it. Automatically. Without anyone drawing a single red line.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2022-06 — Menlo Park, California — and everywhere]

The Lookalike Audience

Facebook's advertising platform was built on a powerful concept: precision targeting. You could tell Facebook exactly who you wanted to reach — by age, by interest, by location, by behavior. And if you didn't know exactly who you wanted, Facebook would help. Upload a list of your best customers, and Facebook would find "lookalike audiences" — people who resembled them in ways Facebook's algorithm had identified.

For most advertisers, this was just good marketing.

For housing advertisers, it was a civil rights violation.

In 2016, ProPublica reporter Julia Angwin demonstrated that Facebook allowed housing advertisers to exclude users by "Ethnic Affinity" — a category Facebook had created based on the content users engaged with. An advertiser could reach "people interested in housing" and then exclude people with an affinity for African American, Hispanic, or Asian American culture.

Facebook initially defended the feature. Then, under pressure, said they would fix it. Then, two years later, the US Department of Housing and Urban Development filed a formal complaint: Facebook was still enabling housing discrimination.

"This is twenty-first century redlining. Facebook is using 21st-century technology to engage in the same type of discriminatory practices that have been illegal for decades."
— Anna Maria Farías, Assistant Secretary for Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity, HUD, 2019

[How the Algorithm Did It Without Trying]

Here is what makes this case different from a human drawing a red line on a map: Facebook's algorithm wasn't targeting by race. It was targeting by interest, behavior, and similarity.

But interest, behavior, and similarity are proxies for race in a country where culture and race are deeply intertwined.

When a housing advertiser used Facebook's "lookalike audience" tool — just targeting people who looked like their current customers — and their current customers were predominantly white, the algorithm found more white prospects. Not because it was told to. Because that's what "people like your customers" meant.

The ACLU ran tests. They created housing ads targeting users within commuting distance of Detroit. They specifically excluded users interested in "African American culture," "Hispanic culture," and similar categories. Facebook approved the ads in minutes.

The ads never needed to say "no minorities." The algorithm handled it automatically.

In March 2022, Facebook (by then Meta) reached a landmark settlement with the Department of Justice, HUD, and civil rights organizations. They agreed to overhaul their entire ad targeting system for housing, employment, and credit — dismantling the tools that had enabled discrimination and building new systems with civil rights protections built in.

The fine: $115,054. For a company worth hundreds of billions of dollars.

[THE FINDING]
Facebook's ad platform did not set out to discriminate. It set out to optimize. Optimization for 'similar users' in a segregated society produces segregated results. The tool did exactly what it was designed to do. That was the problem.

[Why This Is an HR Story]

You might be wondering: why is a housing ad case in an HR governance curriculum?

Because Facebook's employment advertising worked the same way.

The same tools that allowed housing advertisers to exclude by ethnicity also allowed job advertisers to target by age, gender, and zip code. An employer could post a job ad and use Facebook's targeting to show it only to men, only to people under 40, only to people in predominantly white zip codes.

In 2019, the EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission — the federal agency that enforces workplace anti-discrimination laws), the ACLU, and other organizations reached settlements with Facebook that specifically addressed employment ad discrimination. Companies including Amazon, T-Mobile, and Goldman Sachs were individually named in complaints for using Facebook's tools to run age- or gender-targeted job ads.

The platform that connects employers to candidates was silently filtering who got to see the opportunity.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

Facebook's advertising team mapped their product from one perspective: the advertiser's. Revenue optimization. Audience targeting precision. Advertiser satisfaction.

ICM's observe-before-you-build principle requires mapping the process from every perspective that matters — including the perspective of people who will never see the ad because the system decided not to show it to them.

Applying 60/30/10 to the ad targeting product:

— Code (60%): the targeting engine, lookalike audience matching, delivery optimization. All code. All fine in isolation.
— Rules (30%): this is where the system failed completely. The required rules were not written: "housing, employment, and credit advertisers may not exclude users by any attribute that functions as a proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin, or other protected class." That rule existed in federal law for decades. It just never became a product requirement.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): the targeting algorithm's match suggestions are a bounded signal, not an unreviewed outcome. Capped at 10% of the decision weight.
— Human operator (above the stack): a policy reviewer evaluates ad categories where civil rights law applies and approves or blocks before delivery.

The "Ethnic Affinity" exclusion feature was a failure of the 30% rules layer. The law already existed. The product team just never translated it into a system constraint. A rules layer that reflects legal obligations — written before the feature ships — would have prevented this. The factory wasn't built before the machine ran.

[KEY TERMS]

Redlining: The historical practice of denying financial services to residents of certain areas based on race. Outlawed in 1968. Facebook's algorithmic targeting was found to replicate its effects.
Lookalike Audience: An advertising feature that finds new users who resemble an existing customer list. When used without civil rights constraints, it can replicate the demographics of existing customers — including their racial composition.
Disparate Impact (advertising): When an ad targeting system produces racially disparate reach — even without explicitly targeting by race — it may violate the Fair Housing Act and Title VII regardless of intent.
Disparate Treatment: Intentional discrimination against a protected class. Different from disparate impact, which can occur without intent. Both are illegal.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Platform Audit]

Your organization uses job advertising platforms — professional networks, search engines, and social platforms among them.

Ask:
1. What targeting options does each platform offer for job ads?
2. Do any of those options allow filtering by age, gender, or geography in ways that could produce disparate impact?
3. Who in your organization reviews job ad targeting before campaigns go live?

If nobody reviews it — your organization may be running discriminatory ads right now without knowing it.
