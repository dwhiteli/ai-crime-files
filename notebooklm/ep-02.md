How computers learned to make decisions — and why that's both remarkable and dangerous

[The Recipe Analogy]

Imagine you want to learn to make bread. You find 10,000 recipes and read all of them. You notice that nearly every recipe includes flour, water, and yeast. You conclude: bread requires these three things.

You learned a rule — not because someone told you, but because you found the pattern.

That's machine learning. Except instead of recipes, the computer reads millions of resumes. Or loan applications. Or medical records. It looks for patterns — "people who got hired looked like this," "loans that got repaid came from applicants like this" — and applies those patterns to new situations.

Find the pattern. Apply it. That's the machine.

The problem: patterns from the past carry the decisions of the past. And the decisions of the past weren't always fair.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2018-11 — Seattle, Washington]

The Resume Killer

In 2014, Amazon's recruiters had a problem: too many resumes, not enough time.

So they built an AI. The idea was simple: feed the system 10 years of resumes from people who actually got hired, and let it learn what a good candidate looks like.

By 2017, something seemed off.

Researchers reviewing the system's recommendations found it was systematically downgrading resumes from women.

Not because it was told to. Because it had learned to.

The 10 years of hiring data it trained on reflected a decade of tech industry hiring that overwhelmingly favored men. When the system saw the phrase "women's engineering society," it learned to rank that resume lower. Because historically, candidates with that phrase weren't the ones who got hired.

The mirror showed them exactly who they had been.

Amazon scrapped the project in 2018.

"It was never going to be neutral. You're asking it to replicate historical hiring decisions. Historical hiring decisions weren't neutral."
— Amazon engineer working on the project, reported by Reuters

[THE MECHANISM]
AI doesn't decide to be biased. It learns to replicate the past. If the past was biased — and it was — the AI faithfully reproduces that bias at massive scale, with an air of scientific objectivity that makes it harder to challenge.

[The Three Things You Need to Know]

You don't need to understand how to build AI. You need to understand what it does. Here are the only three things that matter for governance:

1. AI finds patterns. It doesn't understand meaning. It can't tell you why something is a pattern — only that it is one.

2. Patterns come from historical data. Historical data reflects historical decisions.

3. Historical decisions often reflected bias, discrimination, or just bad judgment.

Therefore: AI can encode and amplify the mistakes of the past — faster, at greater scale, with an authority that makes them harder to question.

That's the case we're building.

[The Autocorrect Test]

Pull out your phone and try to type something unusual — a specialized term your phone hasn't seen you type before.

Watch what it suggests.

Autocorrect learned from patterns. It's a simplified version of the exact same technology that makes hiring decisions, loan decisions, healthcare decisions.

If the patterns it learned were skewed — even slightly, even unintentionally — it doesn't know. It just corrects.

Now scale that to a decision about whether you get a job interview. Or a mortgage. Or a health diagnosis.

That's why this matters.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

ICM — Interpretable Context Methodology — has a core principle called "observe before you build": map what actually happens when a human makes this decision before you automate it. Watch the human first. Design second.

Amazon's team skipped this step. They never asked: when a human recruiter makes a good hire, what are they actually evaluating — and is our historical hiring data a valid record of those evaluations, or a record of the biases that came with them?

If they had observed before they built, they would have found that 10 years of hiring decisions in tech were not a neutral signal. The pattern they were about to train the algorithm on was already compromised at the source.

The 60/30/10 principle applies here too: 60% of the hiring process can be deterministic (did they meet the qualifications?), 30% can be rules-based (do they pass the structured screen?), but the remaining 10% — the human judgment about potential and fit — cannot be learned from historical data that encoded historical bias. That 10% is where the algorithm tried to go. Observe-before-you-build would have caught that before any code was written.

[KEY TERMS]

Machine Learning: A type of AI that learns patterns from historical data rather than being explicitly programmed with rules. The patterns it learns reflect the data it was trained on.
Training Data: The historical information an AI system learns from. The quality and fairness of training data directly determines whether the AI's decisions will be fair.
Bias (in AI): When an AI system produces systematically unfair results for certain groups — not because it was programmed to discriminate, but because it learned to replicate historical patterns that were already unfair.
Algorithm: A set of rules or instructions a computer follows to make a decision. Can be simple (if X, do Y) or complex (trained AI weighing thousands of factors).

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Pattern Hunt]

Pick one AI-powered tool you use regularly — your phone's recommendations, an email filter, a navigation app.

Write down:
1. What decisions does it make for you?
2. What data did it probably learn from?
3. Who would be well-represented in that historical data? Who might be underrepresented?
4. Is there any way to tell when it's wrong?

You just performed your first bias audit.
