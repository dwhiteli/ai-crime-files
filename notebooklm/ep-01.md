How AI already runs your life — and you didn't know you were a suspect

[The Scene of the Crime]

Before you opened this, AI had already made decisions about you.

Your phone decided which notifications were urgent enough to interrupt you. Your email filtered out messages before you ever saw them. Your music app guessed what you were in the mood for. Your GPS chose your route. If you searched anything this morning, an algorithm — a set of instructions a computer follows to make a decision — decided what you'd see first. And what you'd never see at all.

You weren't asked. You didn't consent. You probably didn't even notice.

That's not a complaint. Most of those decisions were probably fine.

But here's the question that will anchor the next 30 days: What happens when they're not?

[CASE FILE — CASE #2021-07 — Baltimore, Maryland]

The Warehouse Worker

His name is Eric. He worked at an Amazon fulfillment center — one of the enormous warehouses that makes two-day shipping possible. Every day he clocked in, badge scanned, worked his shift.

The warehouse had a system. It tracked everything: how fast you moved, how many packages you processed, how many seconds passed between tasks.

On a Tuesday afternoon, the system decided Eric wasn't working fast enough.

A termination notice printed out.

No manager reviewed it. No human saw it before it happened. The system had logged too many "time off task" violations — including 47 seconds Eric spent getting a drink of water.

Eric was fired by a machine.

"The system would automatically generate the warnings and the firings. Humans weren't really in the loop."
— Former Amazon operations manager, speaking to The Verge

[But Wait — Wasn't That Just Automation?]

You might be thinking: that's not really AI. That's just a computer program following rules.

Here's the thing: that distinction doesn't matter to Eric.

Whether it was a sophisticated learning algorithm or a simple tracking script — a system made a decision about a human being's livelihood without human judgment, human review, or human accountability.

Nobody was responsible. Everyone pointed at the software.

That's the crime. And it's the crime this course is about.

[THE DEFINITION]
AI governance is knowing who is responsible when a machine makes a wrong decision. Everything else — the regulations, the frameworks, the policies, the committees — exists to answer that one question.

[Why You, Why Now]

You might be wondering why you're learning this. You're not an engineer. You're not a lawyer. You don't write code.

Here's why it matters: the people closest to how work actually happens — how decisions get made, what "normal" looks like — are often the first to notice when something is wrong.

The engineers who build these systems often can't see what you can. They're too deep in the code. The executives who approve them are too far from the day-to-day.

You're in the middle. And in AI governance, the middle is where the real work happens.

By Day 30, you'll be able to ask the questions that catch problems before they become cases.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

ICM — Interpretable Context Methodology — is a discipline for building AI workflows that don't over-rely on AI judgment. The central principle is 60/30/10: 60% of a workflow's steps should be straightforward computer logic (code that simply counts and calculates), 30% explicit written rules, and only 10% actual AI judgment.

Applied to Eric's case: Amazon handed 100% of the termination decision to the algorithm. Under 60/30/10, the flow should have been:

— Code (60%): track time data, calculate metrics, flag thresholds. This part was fine.
— Rules (30%): if any accommodation request is on file → mandatory human review before action. If a time-off-task event occurred during a documented medical condition → exempt from automated penalty. That rule would have protected Eric. It just needed to be written down before the system went live.
— Judgment (10%): a manager reviews flagged cases and makes the final call.

The harm wasn't that the tracking code existed. It was that the 30% rules layer — the accommodation rule — was never built. ICM asks before every deployment: what explicit rules protect people the algorithm can't see? We'll apply that question to every case in this curriculum.

[KEY TERMS]

Artificial Intelligence (AI): A computer system that makes decisions based on patterns learned from historical data — rather than following a fixed set of rules written by a programmer.
Automated Decision System: Software that makes or substantially influences decisions about people — hiring, firing, lending, healthcare — without requiring a human to review each one.
AI Governance: The policies, processes, and oversight structures that establish who is responsible when an AI system makes a decision — especially a wrong one.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — Your First Investigation]

Think about three decisions that affect your work — when you get a notification, how calendar invites are prioritized, how expense reports get routed.

For each one, write down:
1. Who actually makes this decision — a person, or a system?
2. If it's a system: do you know who would fix it if it made a mistake?
3. If you don't know who's responsible — you've found your first governance gap.

This is the governance audit. You just ran your first one.
