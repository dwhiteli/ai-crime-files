[The Invisible Infrastructure]

Before you can govern it, you have to find it.

Most HR leaders, when asked how many AI systems their organization uses, guess a number. The actual number is almost always higher — often dramatically higher.

This is not because people are careless. It's because AI has become infrastructure. It's embedded in platforms you already use. It arrives under SaaS licenses. It's described in product updates as 'enhanced capabilities' and 'intelligent features.' It's turned on by default.

A 2023 survey found that 83% of organizations using AI in HR couldn't provide a complete inventory of those systems. Not because they didn't have the systems — but because nobody had written them all down.

You can't govern what you haven't mapped.

[CASE FILE — CASE FILE — DISCOVERY — U.S. Federal Courts, Ongoing]

The Litigation Discovery Problem

When a lawsuit involves AI-assisted decisions, the first thing opposing counsel does is ask for the inventory.

They send a broad discovery request: every AI tool, every algorithm, every automated system involved in any decision affecting the plaintiffs.

The HR department scrambles. IT starts pulling vendor contracts. Someone finds a Slack message about a product that was piloted eighteen months ago. The software company gets subpoenaed.

In multiple cases involving AI discrimination claims, the inventory that emerged during discovery was larger than what HR could have produced before the lawsuit.

This is not a hypothetical. In the Mobley v. Workday case, in EEOC investigations, in state agency audits — the pattern recurs. Companies using AI tools in hiring and HR decisions are often unable to produce a complete, accurate list of those tools on short notice.

The lesson: your AI inventory shouldn't be something you build under subpoena. It should be something you maintain continuously, so you can answer any question — from an employee, a regulator, or a judge — with the same answer you'd give on a Tuesday morning when nothing is on fire.

[THE FOUR PLACES AI HIDES]
1. PURPOSE-BUILT HR AI TOOLS — The systems you bought specifically for AI capabilities: ATS scoring, video interview analysis, performance feedback generation, flight risk prediction.

2. PLATFORM-EMBEDDED AI — Features inside tools you already have: HRIS predictive analytics, scheduling optimization, attrition forecasting built into your engagement platform. These often get turned on during a product update.

3. SHADOW AI — Tools individual employees or teams adopted without formal approval: ChatGPT for writing performance reviews, AI writing assistants for offer letters, individual subscriptions to scoring tools.

4. LEGACY AI — Systems built or deployed years ago that are no longer actively monitored but are still running: old rule-based scoring systems, recommendation engines in talent marketplaces, automated filtering logic nobody updated when the job requirements changed.

Your inventory is only complete when it covers all four categories. Most programs only know about category one.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

The AI inventory exercise is ICM's observe-before-you-build principle applied retroactively — because most organizations deployed the code without observing first, and now need to understand what they actually built.

For each significant HR process — sourcing, screening, hiring, onboarding, performance, compensation, promotion, termination, monitoring — you ask three people:

The HR owner: what did we buy?
The technology owner: what did we deploy and what features are active?
The employee or manager closest to the decision: what are you actually using?

You only have a complete picture when all three answers match. They almost never match on the first round.

Applying 60/30/10 to the inventory gap:
— Code (60%): the tools that are running right now — including platform-embedded features and shadow AI that HR didn't consciously adopt.
— Rules (30%): for each tool, do rules exist governing when it may be used, what decisions it may influence, and when a human must intervene? In most organizations, the inventory reveals that the code layer far exceeds the rules layer.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): for each tool, what share of the decision does the AI actually carry today, and is that share bounded — or has it crept past the 10% ceiling into the whole decision?
— Human operator (above the stack): the named owner who is accountable for each system's behavior — applying the rules, weighing the bounded AI signal, owning the outcome.

The gaps between what was bought, what was deployed, and what rules govern its use: that's your governance inventory problem. Map the code before you can build the rules around it.

[KEY TERMS]

AI System Inventory: A comprehensive catalog of all artificial intelligence and automated decision-making systems used by an organization, including purpose-built tools, platform features, and legacy systems. The foundation of any governance program — you cannot manage what you haven't documented.
Shadow AI: AI tools adopted by employees or teams outside of official IT and HR procurement processes. Often represents the largest gap in organizational AI inventories, particularly as AI becomes embedded in everyday productivity tools.
Risk Tier (AI): A classification of an AI system based on the significance of the decisions it influences. High risk: hiring, promotion, pay, termination. Medium risk: scheduling, monitoring, benefits navigation. Low risk: document drafting, information retrieval. Tier determines the level of oversight required.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — Map Your Stack]

Start your AI inventory — even if it's just for one HR process you own.

Pick one process: hiring, performance reviews, compensation, engagement — something you're directly involved in.

For that process, answer:
1. What automated systems are involved at any step?
2. For each system: what decision does it influence?
3. For each system: who approved its use, and when?
4. For each system: when was it last reviewed for accuracy or fairness?
5. What would you find if you asked every individual involved what tools they're using personally?

When you finish, estimate: if you did this exercise for every HR process in your organization, how long would it take?

That duration tells you something important about your governance readiness.
