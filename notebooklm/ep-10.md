Case #10: The Firing Line

When AI recommendations become the mechanism for workforce reductions — and nobody owns the decision

Listen time: 30 minutes

[The 30-Second Approval]

Imagine you're an HR manager at a large company. You have a stack of 40 performance review cases to process this week. The system has already ranked them. Flagged the low performers. Suggested which roles to eliminate in the next restructuring.

You click through the cases. Each one takes about 30 seconds. You review the score, confirm the recommendation, move on.

At the end of the day, you've "reviewed" 40 cases. But have you actually reviewed them? Or have you clicked approve on whatever the algorithm recommended?

This is the question at the center of AI-assisted terminations — and it may be the most important governance question in HR today. If a human approves an AI recommendation in 30 seconds without reading the file, did a human make that decision?

The answer, legally and ethically, matters enormously.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2018-03 — Armonk, New York — and everywhere IBM operates]

The IBM Restructuring

In March 2018, ProPublica reporters Peter Gosselin and Ariana Tobin published an investigation into IBM's workforce strategy.

Over the previous five years, IBM had laid off tens of thousands of workers. The reporters documented that these layoffs had disproportionately affected older employees. Between 2013 and 2018, more than 85% of those targeted for layoff were workers over 40. ProPublica estimated IBM had eliminated over 20,000 American employees aged 40 or over in just five years.

Internal emails obtained by investigators used strikingly candid language. Older workers were referred to as 'dinobabies' who needed to be made 'extinct,' as 'gray hairs' who were 'not digital natives,' and one email described a 'dated maternal workforce' that didn't 'understand social or engagement.'

IBM had developed and sold AI-powered HR tools — including systems called 'Watson Candidate Assistant' and platforms for 'workforce rebalancing,' the corporate euphemism for deciding who to cut. The company selling AI HR solutions to the world was using AI internally to manage its own workforce reductions.

The EEOC's August 2020 letter of determination confirmed the allegations: IBM's messaging from 'the highest ranks' directed managers to reduce headcount among older workers to make room for 'early professional hires.' Former employees filed hundreds of ADEA complaints. But IBM had a defense: arbitration contracts — agreements employees had signed that required them to resolve disputes through a private process rather than in public court — that forced each individual worker into a secret, private proceeding. In August 2023, the Second Circuit upheld those contracts.

Then, on April 16, 2026 — eight days before this curriculum was last updated — the Department of Justice announced IBM would pay $17,077,043 to resolve separate allegations that it had used illegal demographic targets in hiring and promotion decisions.

The core question that survives every settlement: when an AI system recommends which employees to cut, and a manager approves that recommendation in a 30-second workflow review, who made the decision to end that person's career?

"IBM has mastered the art of laying off older workers so that it can be defended later as a routine business decision. The tools they are selling to help other companies do this are the same tools they are using on their own people."
— Peter Gosselin, ProPublica, 2018

[The Amazon Pipeline]

Amazon's approach to performance management is among the most automated in corporate history.

The system — sometimes called "the machine" internally — tracks hundreds of performance metrics for warehouse workers: units processed per hour, time off task, error rates, attendance. It generates automated warnings. It generates performance improvement plans. And in some cases, it generates termination recommendations.

What Amazon has built is essentially a pipeline: data flows in, performance scores are calculated, recommendations are generated, and — depending on the threshold — automated actions are taken. At the human end of that pipeline, managers may approve or review actions. But the rhythm of the system, the volume of cases, and the framing of the recommendation all push toward approval.

This is what governance researchers call automation bias: the human tendency to trust and defer to automated recommendations, especially when the system is presented as objective and the human reviewer is busy.

Automation bias isn't laziness. It's a predictable human response to being embedded in a system designed around the assumption that the algorithm is right.

[THE FINDING]
When AI systems are embedded into termination workflows, two risks compound each other. First, the algorithm may have learned from biased historical data — replicating past patterns of who gets fired. Second, automation bias means human reviewers are likely to approve algorithmic recommendations without meaningful scrutiny. The result: discriminatory patterns execute at scale, with a human signature that provides legal cover.

[The Legal Question Nobody Has Fully Answered]

Here is where AI-assisted terminations sit legally right now: in unresolved territory.

Title VII (the Civil Rights Act, which prohibits discrimination based on race, sex, and other characteristics), the ADEA (Age Discrimination in Employment Act, which protects workers 40 and older), and the ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act, which protects qualified individuals with disabilities) all prohibit employment discrimination. They apply regardless of whether the discriminatory decision was made by a human or an algorithm. Buying a discriminatory tool doesn't transfer liability to the vendor — the employer remains responsible.

But proving that an AI-assisted termination was discriminatory is hard. The algorithm's methodology is often proprietary. The manager's approval is documented. The causal chain — from biased training data to discriminatory recommendation to human approval — is difficult to establish in court.

The EEOC has been increasingly clear: it will apply existing civil rights law to AI-assisted HR decisions. The agency's 2023 guidance specifically addresses AI in employment and makes clear that employers cannot use algorithmic tools to evade anti-discrimination obligations.

The law is catching up. The governance structures that would have prevented the harm are still being built.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

This episode ends Phase II, and it ends with the ICM question that connects all seven cases through the lens of 60/30/10.

In every case we've studied, the same breakdown occurred: the code was built (60%), the rules layer was skipped or left vague (30%), the AI's 10% ceiling was breached and became the entire decision, and the human operator above the stack was either absent or reduced to a ceremonial rubber-stamp.

At IBM and Amazon, the termination workflow's rules layer should have included: "a reviewer must document specific context they considered beyond the algorithm's output," "any recommendation affecting a worker over 40 must include an age-impact note," and "a review that takes fewer than X minutes per case is automatically flagged for quality review." Without those rules, the human reviewer becomes a signature on an algorithmic decision — not a genuine check.

The question ICM requires before any termination-adjacent AI goes live: "When this system makes a recommendation that affects someone's employment, what human review happens before action is taken — and is that review meaningful, or ceremonial?"

A meaningful review is one where the reviewer has time, information, and authority to say no. That condition is created by the rules layer, not by hoping reviewers will be diligent.

Every framework you'll study in Phase III — NIST, EU AI Act, EEOC guidance — is trying to enforce this same principle: the 30% rules layer must be built before the system touches a human life.

[KEY TERMS]

Automation Bias: The tendency for humans to over-trust automated recommendations, especially in high-volume environments. A major risk in AI-assisted HR decisions where humans are nominally in the loop but functionally deferring to the algorithm.
Meaningful Human Review: Oversight that is substantive, not ceremonial — where the reviewer has time, authority, information, and genuine ability to override the AI recommendation. Distinguished from 'rubber stamp' approval.
ADEA: Age Discrimination in Employment Act. Prohibits employment discrimination against workers 40 and older. Applies to AI-assisted decisions the same as human decisions.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Meaningful Review Test]

Think about one place in your organization where a human approves an automated recommendation — a performance flag, a candidate screen, a compensation adjustment.

Ask honestly:
1. How long does that review typically take?
2. What information does the reviewer actually look at?
3. In the last year, how often has a reviewer overridden the system's recommendation?

If overrides are rare, the review may be ceremonial rather than meaningful. That's not a criticism of the reviewer — it's a governance design problem.
