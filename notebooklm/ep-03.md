The single governance concept everything else builds on — and why most organizations don't have it

[The Building With No Fire Marshal]

Imagine a large office building. Beautiful offices. Multiple fire exits. Smoke detectors on every floor. Fire extinguishers in the hallways. An automatic sprinkler system.

Now: who is the fire marshal?

Building management says it's the facilities team. The facilities team says it's building management. Building management says it's HR. HR has never heard this was their responsibility.

One afternoon, a small fire starts in the server room. Everyone evacuates safely.

Then they stand outside and realize: nobody called the fire department. Because nobody knew whose job that was.

The equipment was perfect. The ownership was missing.

That's most organizations' relationship with their AI.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2019-09 — United States, nationwide]

Nobody Owned the Algorithm

In 2019, researchers at UC Berkeley made a disturbing discovery.

A commercial algorithm used by hospitals and insurance companies across the country — to determine which patients needed extra healthcare support — was systematically under-assigning care to Black patients.

For the same health status, the algorithm scored Black patients as needing less care than white patients. Researchers estimated it affected tens of millions of patients per year.

The company said the bias wasn't intentional. And they were right. The algorithm had used healthcare costs as a proxy for healthcare needs. Because of systemic inequality, Black patients historically had less access to care — meaning they spent less on healthcare even when they were equally sick. The algorithm learned that lower costs meant lower needs.

It faithfully replicated a historical injustice.

But here's what investigators found most alarming: when they asked who owned this algorithm — who monitored its fairness, who had authority to change it, who would be accountable if it caused harm — there was no clear answer.

An algorithm making healthcare decisions for tens of millions of people had no identified owner.

"The study represents an important moment. It shows that health care algorithms can have significant racial bias — and companies may not know it."
— The New York Times, on the Obermeyer et al. study, published in Science, 2019

[THE PATTERN]
In almost every AI governance failure, there's a moment where investigators ask: who owned this system? And the answer is either nobody, or everyone pointing at someone else. The technology was sophisticated. The accountability was absent.

[The Wernfeldt Model]

A data governance consultant named John Wernfeldt has been making an important argument: governance isn't documentation. It isn't policy. It's clarity about who makes decisions.

He describes three layers every organization needs:

Layer 1 — The Facts: Your data, your systems, the source of truth.
Layer 2 — The Quality Controls: Making sure the facts are accurate and reliable.
Layer 3 — Decision Authority: Who owns a metric? Who can change a definition? Who resolves conflicts?

His argument: if ownership is unclear at Layer 3, nothing above it scales. You can have the most sophisticated AI in the world. If nobody owns the algorithm's behavior — who trained it, who monitors it, who can shut it down — you don't have AI governance.

You have a building with no fire marshal.

[The Three Questions That Anchor Everything]

Every governance framework we'll study is answering three questions:

Who decides? — Ownership. A specific person's name attached to this system's behavior.

Who reviews? — Oversight. A process that catches problems before they become cases.

Who's accountable when it goes wrong? — The person who can't say "that was the algorithm's fault."

That's it. NIST frameworks, EU regulations, corporate policies — all of it is scaffolding around these three questions.

If you can answer all three for every AI system in your organization, you have governance.

If you can't — you have a building with no fire marshal.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

ICM — Interpretable Context Methodology — calls this the "factory first" principle: build the governance structure before running the system, not after complaints arrive.

The healthcare algorithm in this case had no owner because nobody built the ownership structure before it went live. The factory — the rules, the oversight roles, the appeal paths — was never assembled. The machine ran with no one minding it.

Applied to the 60/30/10 principle: the data collection and scoring was code (60%). The rules layer (30%) — "who reviews this algorithm's fairness annually?" "what threshold of racial disparity triggers a pause?" "who is the named accountability owner?" — those rules were never written. So 100% of consequential decisions fell to the algorithm, with 0% rules and 0% human judgment attached.

Two questions ICM requires before any AI system goes live:

"If this system makes a wrong decision, what is the specific process to catch it and correct it?"

"Who is the named person responsible for this system's behavior?"

If nobody in the room can answer either question with a specific name and a specific process — you've found the crime scene before the crime happened. The factory wasn't built. That's the governance failure.

[KEY TERMS]

Governance: The system that determines who has decision-making authority, who provides oversight, and who is accountable for outcomes. Not a document — a living structure of responsibility.
Data Governance: Who owns data, who can change definitions, who resolves conflicts about what data means. The foundation that AI governance builds on.
Decision Authority: The explicit assignment of who has the final say on a given decision — including decisions made by automated systems. The most frequently missing element in AI governance.
ICM (Interpretable Context Methodology): A discipline for designing AI workflows that don't over-rely on AI judgment. The 60/30/10 principle: 60% of a workflow's steps should be straightforward computer logic that simply counts and calculates (code), 30% should be explicit written rules, and only 10% should be AI judgment. When an organization skips the rules layer (30%), consequential decisions get handed entirely to the algorithm. Also applies the 'factory first' principle: build governance structure — rules, oversight, appeal paths — before running the system, not after complaints arrive.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Ownership Audit]

Identify one automated system in your organization — an expense approval workflow, a scheduling system, anything that makes decisions without asking a person every time.

Ask yourself:
1. Who owns it? A specific person's name, not a team.
2. What happens when it makes a mistake? A specific process, not "IT handles it."
3. When was it last reviewed for fairness or accuracy?

If you can't answer all three — that's not a technology problem. That's a governance problem.

You now know what one looks like.
