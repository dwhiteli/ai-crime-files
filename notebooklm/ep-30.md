[YOUR DELIVERABLE — THE CAPSTONE PACKAGE]
Submit one capstone package with a main memo and three appendices:

MAIN MEMO — A board-ready governance assessment of 1,800 to 2,200 words, addressed to the CHRO and General Counsel, written so it can be repurposed for the Human Capital and Risk Committee. Structure it using the six sections below.

APPENDIX A — A one-page AI system register covering all eight tools, with: owner, use case, decision affected, geography, data type, risk level, and your keep / pause / narrow / retire recommendation.

APPENDIX B — A ninety-day remediation roadmap with owner, deadline, and decision point for each major action.

APPENDIX C — A one-page recommendation on whether to renew InterviewVue Insight, renew with conditions, or terminate and replace with a temporary manual process.

THE SIX MEMO SECTIONS:
1. Executive Diagnosis — State plainly whether SummitPeak's current posture is governable, what the three most urgent risks are, and what executive decisions are needed this week.
2. System Inventory and Decision Map — Classify each system by what decision it influences, who uses it, where, and whether it is advisory or materially influential.
3. Legal and Regulatory Map — Which systems create immediate legal exposure, near-term regulatory exposure, or internal policy and trust exposure.
4. Controls and Evidence Review — For each higher-risk system: what evidence exists and what is missing. Validation, bias audit, notice, accommodation process, human review, logging, vendor change terms, data handling, incident response, appeal path.
5. Risk Treatment Decisions — Keep, keep with conditions, narrow, pause, or retire. Where you recommend continued use, specify the condition that must be met first.
6. Operating Model and Board Reporting — Who owns intake, who approves use, what documentation is mandatory, what monitoring occurs, what triggers escalation, and what the board receives quarterly.

"The difference between good governance and checkbox compliance is judgment. A good answer knows that a bias audit is useful but not sufficient. It knows the employer is accountable even when the vendor supplied the tool. It notices the issue that is approaching even if no regulator has raised it yet."

[THE FIVE QUESTIONS YOUR MEMO MUST ANSWER]
1. WHICH SYSTEMS ARE MATERIALLY INFLUENTIAL? — Do not treat all eight as equal. Show that you understand the difference between a lower-stakes assistant and a tool sitting inside hiring, promotion, pay, or retention decisions.

2. WHAT ARE THE THREE MOST URGENT EXPOSURES? — Include at least one issue that is not yet the subject of an active complaint. You are expected to spot the visible fires and the approaching one.

3. WHICH SYSTEMS SHOULD BE PAUSED OR NARROWED? — 'Human in the loop' is not enough. Specify who the human is, what authority they have, and what must exist before continued use is allowed.

4. SHOULD SUMMITPEAK RENEW INTERVIERVUE INSIGHT? — Pick one position: renew, renew with conditions, or do not renew. State the exact evidence, contract changes, and interim hiring controls your position requires.

5. WHAT GOVERNANCE OPERATING MODEL PREVENTS THIS FROM RECURRING? — Name the forum, the core functions, the intake standard, the required artifacts, and the monitoring cadence. The board should receive oversight, not a one-time clean-up memo.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

ICM CLOSING REFLECTION — The Final Investigation

After you complete your governance memo, write one final reflection of 250 to 400 words answering this question:

For three of the eight SummitPeak AI systems, apply the ICM principles specifically — not generically.

For each of your three examples, address all three ICM principles:

1. Observe before you build: what human decision-making process should have been observed before this tool was deployed — and what would that observation have revealed about a risk the tool created?

2. 60/30/10: what specific rules (the 30% layer) were missing for this tool — rules that, if written before launch, would have prevented the specific problem that materialized? Where was the AI's 10% ceiling breached? Who should have been the named human operator above the stack?

3. Factory first: what governance structure should have existed before this tool went live — and who should have been in the room that wasn't?

A strong reflection will not say 'they needed more governance.'

It will say, with precision: which front-end observation was skipped, which rule was never written, which governance structure was never built — and how each absence connects directly to a specific harm in the SummitPeak scenario.

ICM — Interpretable Context Methodology — is not a checklist. It is a discipline: the practice of refusing to run the code until the rules layer exists, the AI's 10% ceiling is bounded, a named human operator stands above the stack, and the factory is standing.

For 30 days, you've studied what happens when organizations skip that discipline.

Now you know what to ask. You know who needs to be in the room. You know what a governance program looks like when it has authority, not just documentation.

The file is open. Close it well.

[KEY TERMS]

Governance Assessment: A structured evaluation of an organization's AI governance posture — covering inventory, legal exposure, control gaps, risk classification, and operating model. Different from a compliance audit (which checks rules were followed) in that it evaluates whether the underlying governance structure is adequate to prevent future harm.
Remediation Roadmap: A time-phased action plan for closing governance gaps, typically organized into immediate (0–30 days), near-term (31–60 days), and structured (61–90 days) horizons. Each action should have an owner, deadline, and a defined decision point — not just a target completion date.
Board-Ready Communication: A document or presentation designed for a board committee audience: decision-focused rather than descriptive, risk-ranked rather than comprehensive, specific about accountability rather than general about policy. A board-ready governance assessment gives the board a decision, a risk view, and a governance cadence — not a narrative of events.
