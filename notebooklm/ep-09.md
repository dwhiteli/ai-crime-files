[CASE FILE — CASE #2019-10 — Published in Science, October 25, 2019]

The Obermeyer Study

Ziad Obermeyer is a physician and researcher at the University of California Berkeley. In 2019, he and three colleagues — Brian Powers, Christine Vogeli, and Sendhil Mullainathan — published a study in Science, one of the most prestigious scientific journals in the world.

The paper's title was clinical: 'Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations.'

The subject was a commercial algorithm widely used by hospitals and insurance companies across the United States to identify which patients needed enrollment in "care management programs" — intensive support for patients with complex chronic conditions. These programs meant more check-in calls, more specialist referrals, more preventive interventions.

The algorithm scored millions of patients. The scores determined who got extra care.

Obermeyer's team had obtained data on the algorithm's predictions and compared them to patients' actual health status. What they found was that the algorithm was systematically wrong — and wrong in a specific direction.

"At a given risk score, Black patients are considerably sicker than White patients. The magnitude of the bias is striking: at the algorithm's decision threshold for extra care, Black patients have significantly more chronic conditions than White patients."
— Obermeyer et al., Science, October 2019

[The Flaw in the Formula]

The algorithm used healthcare costs as a proxy for healthcare needs. The logic seemed reasonable: sicker patients generally have higher medical costs, so cost is a good predictor of how much care someone needs.

Except it wasn't. Not equally. Not across race.

Black patients, on average, spent less on healthcare than white patients with the same level of illness. Not because they needed less care — because they had received less care. Decades of unequal access to healthcare, distrust of the medical system, economic barriers, and geographic gaps in healthcare availability had produced lower historical costs for Black patients — even when their underlying health was equally or more compromised.

The algorithm learned from cost data. Cost data reflected historical inequity. The algorithm learned to reproduce that inequity.

At the same risk score threshold, the researchers found that Black patients had significantly more chronic conditions than white patients. The algorithm was, in effect, requiring Black patients to be sicker than white patients to qualify for the same level of care.

The researchers estimated that the algorithm affected approximately 200 million people per year in the United States.

[THE NUMBERS]
The researchers found that if the bias were corrected, the percentage of Black patients identified as needing extra care would increase from 17.7% to 46.5%. The algorithm had been cutting the number of Black patients who qualified for intensive care management by nearly two-thirds.

[The Fix Was Simple. The Governance Question Was Hard.]

Here is what makes this case particularly striking: the fix was relatively straightforward.

The algorithm was using cost as a proxy for health. Obermeyer's team showed that if you replaced cost with a direct measure of health — actual number of chronic conditions, lab test results — the racial disparity largely disappeared.

The company behind the algorithm, Optum (a subsidiary of UnitedHealth Group), worked with Obermeyer's team after publication. They acknowledged the finding and committed to changes.

But the hard questions remained.

The algorithm had been running for years. Hundreds of millions of patients had been scored. An unknown number of Black patients had been denied access to care management programs they should have qualified for. What happened to those patients? What was the health impact of the care they didn't receive? Who was accountable?

Optum did not face regulatory penalties. There were no EEOC complaints. No class action lawsuits have succeeded on this specific case.

The harm was documented, quantified, and published in one of the world's most respected scientific journals. And nothing changed except the algorithm.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

This case contains one of the clearest 60/30/10 failures in the entire curriculum.

Before this algorithm was deployed, someone chose healthcare cost as a proxy for health need. That was a design decision — not inevitable, not the only option. ICM's observe-before-you-build principle would have required mapping how human care managers actually identified high-need patients before automating that judgment.

If they had observed first, they would have found that cost and need are not equivalent across racial groups. Public health researchers had documented this. The knowledge existed. It was never applied to the design.

Applying 60/30/10 to this algorithm:

— Code (60%): collect patient data, calculate cost history, generate a risk score. This was built.
— Rules (30%): required rules that were never written: "any proxy variable must be validated for demographic equivalence before use in a scoring model," and "if selection rates for extra care differ by more than X% across racial groups, the algorithm is paused pending review." These rules were knowable. They just didn't exist.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): the risk score is a bounded signal, capped at 10% of the decision weight. A flag that surfaces a patient for review, not a determination of care need.
— Human operator (above the stack): a clinical reviewer examines flagged cases, applies the rules layer, and owns the care-assignment decision.

The algorithm ran for years affecting an estimated 200 million patients annually. A functioning rules layer — built before the system launched, not discovered by outside researchers years later — would have caught the disparity at the design stage. The factory-first principle requires that oversight structure to exist before the machine runs. It didn't.

[KEY TERMS]

Proxy Variable: A measurable quantity used to represent something harder to measure directly. In healthcare algorithms, cost is often used as a proxy for health need. The validity of proxies must be tested across demographic groups.
Structural Inequality: Systemic patterns of disadvantage embedded in institutions and practices over time. Healthcare algorithms trained on historical data can learn and reproduce these patterns without anyone intending them to.
Disparate Impact (healthcare): When a healthcare algorithm produces systematically different outcomes for patients of different racial groups — even when trained on race-neutral data — it may constitute discrimination under civil rights law.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Proxy Audit]

Think about one metric your organization uses to evaluate people — performance scores, potential ratings, flight risk predictions, anything.

Ask:
1. What is this metric actually measuring directly?
2. What is it being used as a proxy for?
3. Is that proxy equally valid across different demographic groups in your workforce?
4. When was the last time anyone checked?

This is the proxy audit. Most HR metrics have never been subjected to one.
