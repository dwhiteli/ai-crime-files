Case #05: Guilty Until the Algorithm Says Otherwise

COMPAS, recidivism scoring, and the black box that determined prison sentences

Listen time: 35 minutes

[Two People, Two Scores, One Algorithm]

Broward County, Florida. 2013.

Brisha Borden was 18 years old. She and a friend had attempted to ride away on an unlocked bicycle and a child's scooter. They were caught, arrested, and charged with petty theft.

Two blocks away, Vernon Prater had a more serious record — he had been convicted of armed robbery and sentenced to five years in prison years earlier. He'd been caught shoplifting $86.35 worth of tools from Home Depot.

Both were processed through the Broward County courts. Both were evaluated by a risk assessment algorithm called COMPAS.

COMPAS assigned Brisha Borden — the teenager with the bicycle — a high risk score. Future criminal. Likely to reoffend.

COMPAS assigned Vernon Prater — the prior armed robber — a low risk score. Unlikely to reoffend.

Two years later, journalists would check what had actually happened. Borden had been charged with a minor infraction. Prater had been arrested for breaking into a warehouse and stealing thousands of dollars in goods.

The algorithm had gotten both of them exactly backwards.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2016-05 — Broward County, Florida — and 34 other states]

Machine Bias

In May 2016, ProPublica published an investigation that would become one of the most cited pieces of journalism in the history of AI accountability.

Reporters Julia Angwin, Jeff Larson, Surya Mattu, and Lauren Kirchner had done something that almost nobody had done before: they obtained the COMPAS risk scores for more than 7,000 people arrested in Broward County, Florida, and compared them to what actually happened to those people over the following two years.

What they found stopped the country.

Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be falsely flagged as future criminals — labeled high-risk but then not rearrested.

White defendants were more likely to be mistakenly flagged as lower risk and then go on to commit additional crimes.

COMPAS — short for Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions — had been used in 34 states to inform bail, sentencing, and parole decisions. Millions of people had passed through it. Judges had relied on its scores.

"We're not able to tell defendants what's in the algorithm. We don't have access to the algorithm. The company considers that proprietary."
— Broward County judge, during a COMPAS-related court proceeding

[The 137 Questions]

COMPAS asked defendants 137 questions. Race was not one of them.

Instead, it asked about things like: Where did you grow up? What's your family's criminal history? How many times did you move as a child? Do you have friends who have been in trouble with the law? How often do you get angry?

These questions are called "criminogenic factors" — risk indicators that researchers have associated with reoffending.

The problem is that many of these factors are deeply correlated with race in the United States. Zip codes, family incarceration history, educational disruption — these aren't race-neutral variables. They encode the effects of decades of systemic inequality.

COMPAS didn't ask about race. It didn't need to. The proxies were already there.

The company behind COMPAS — Northpointe, later renamed Equivant — disputed the ProPublica findings. They argued that the algorithm had equal predictive accuracy across racial groups. Technically, they were right.

But ProPublica's point wasn't about accuracy. It was about error type. COMPAS made different kinds of errors for Black defendants than for white defendants. For Black defendants, it disproportionately predicted crime that didn't happen. For white defendants, it disproportionately missed crime that did.

Same accuracy rate. Systematically different consequences.

[THE FINDING]
COMPAS scored Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the false-positive rate of white defendants. It scored white defendants as low-risk at nearly twice the false-positive rate of Black defendants. The algorithm was, in effect, assigning risk based on race — without ever asking the question.

[The Black Box Problem]

Here is the part of this story that matters most for AI governance.

Judges using COMPAS scores in sentencing hearings did not know how the score was calculated. Defense attorneys could not challenge the methodology. Defendants could not see the questions that generated their score, nor could they explain why an answer might be misleading in their specific case.

Northpointe considered the algorithm proprietary. It was a trade secret. The justice system — with the power to take away human freedom — was making decisions based on a formula nobody was allowed to examine.

In 2017, a Wisconsin case called Loomis v. Wisconsin reached the state Supreme Court. Eric Loomis argued that using COMPAS in his sentencing violated his right to due process — the constitutional guarantee that a person facing a consequential government decision can understand and challenge the evidence against them. He couldn't challenge a system he couldn't see.

The court ruled against him. The score wasn't the only factor in the decision, they said.

But the question Loomis raised never went away: in a system built on the right to confront evidence against you, how do you confront an algorithm?

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

COMPAS was never subjected to an ICM review before its deployment in courtrooms. The 60/30/10 principle reveals exactly where it failed.

— Code (60%): COMPAS collected data and computed a risk score. That part was built.
— Rules (30%): This is where the failure lives. The rules layer should have included: "this score may not be cited in sentencing without independent human review," "defendants must be informed of the scoring methodology," and "if selection rates across racial groups diverge by more than X%, deployment must pause pending investigation." None of those rules were written before the system went live.
— Judgment (10%): A judge reviews the score as one input among many — not as a number that settles the question.

Instead, COMPAS operated as if the rules layer didn't need to exist. Judges were handed a number with no requirement to question it, defendants had no mechanism to challenge it, and nobody owned the fairness question.

The factory-first principle of ICM would have required the oversight structure — the rules, the appeal paths, the disparity thresholds — to be built before the system touched a single sentencing hearing. They weren't. The harm ran for years before a journalist found it.

[KEY TERMS]

Recidivism: The tendency of a convicted person to reoffend. Risk assessment tools like COMPAS try to predict this — with documented racial disparities.
Algorithmic Accountability: The principle that organizations deploying AI systems must be able to explain, justify, and take responsibility for the outcomes those systems produce.
False Positive / False Negative: A false positive means predicting something that doesn't happen (flagging someone as high-risk who isn't). A false negative means missing something that does (flagging someone as low-risk who reoffends). COMPAS had racially unequal error rates across both types.
Proprietary Algorithm: An AI system whose methodology is protected as a trade secret, preventing external scrutiny. COMPAS's proprietary nature made it impossible for defendants to challenge their scores.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Transparency Test]

Think about one automated decision that affects people at your organization — a hiring screen, a performance rating, a compensation band assignment.

Ask:
1. If an employee asked "how was this decision made about me?" — could you give them a real answer?
2. If they disagreed with the outcome, is there a process to challenge it?
3. If neither answer is yes — what right do they have to appeal a machine's judgment?

You just ran the transparency test. Most organizations fail it.
