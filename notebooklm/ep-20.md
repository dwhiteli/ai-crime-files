Case #20: The Pay Gap Machine

How algorithmic compensation systems can automate historical inequity — and what governance looks like

Listen time: 30 minutes


---

[The Formula That Felt Objective]

For decades, compensation professionals have been moving toward more systematic, data-driven pay decisions — market surveys, salary bands (the pay range for a given role), compa-ratios (a ratio comparing what someone earns to the midpoint of their pay range), and pay equity analyses.

The goal was objectivity. Reduce manager discretion. Reduce the variability that allowed bias to creep in through individual decisions.

AI compensation tools are the logical extension of this trend. An algorithm that processes market data, internal equity factors, performance scores, and role requirements to produce an offer recommendation feels like the ultimate in objective pay-setting.

The problem: what the algorithm calls objective, researchers call encoding the past.

If the market data reflects a labor market where women and people of color have historically been underpaid, the algorithm learns that underpaying them is market-rate. If the internal equity factors include historical pay — the employee's current salary, last salary increase, time in role — they carry every bias that went into those historical numbers.

The algorithm didn't create the pay gap. But deployed without governance, it automates it forward.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2023-07 — United Kingdom — 2023]

The Uber Pay Algorithm Challenge

In 2023, Uber drivers in the UK served legal demands on the company to halt its AI-driven pay system.

The drivers, represented by the App Drivers & Couriers Union, challenged Uber's algorithmic pay-setting on multiple grounds: lack of transparency about how the algorithm calculated earnings, inability to contest algorithmic decisions, and concern that the system produced racially disparate pay outcomes for drivers working similar routes.

The case drew on the UK General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which gives workers rights against solely automated decisions that significantly affect them — including the right to explanation and the right to human review.

Uber's response was to provide more transparency about how rates were calculated.

The case established a pattern that HR professionals in all industries need to understand: algorithmic pay systems are not immune from discrimination law. Workers are developing legal strategies to challenge them. And the GDPR right to explanation — increasingly mirrored in US state laws — requires employers to be able to explain how automated pay decisions are made.

[THE CALIFORNIA STANDARD]
California's Civil Rights Council regulations on automated employment decision systems explicitly include compensation within their scope. An AI system that materially influences pay decisions — offer amounts, merit increases, equity grants — is subject to the same disparate impact analysis as a hiring tool. HR professionals in California are already operating under this standard. It is likely to spread.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

AI compensation tools are the clearest example of the 60/30/10 failure pattern applied to a domain where organizations thought they were being more rigorous, not less.

The observe-before-you-build principle is crucial here: if you mapped how a skilled compensation professional actually makes a pay decision, you would find they consider market data, internal equity, and the specific candidate's context — and they ask whether any of those inputs carries historical bias. AI compensation tools automate the data processing but skip the bias question entirely.

Applying 60/30/10:

— Code (60%): pull market survey data, calculate compa-ratios, generate a recommended range. Fully automatable.
— Rules (30%): required rules that most organizations have never written into their compensation AI: "market data sources must be validated for demographic composition before use," "any offer recommendation that falls below the band midpoint for a candidate in a protected class must be reviewed by Compensation before delivery," and "year-over-year pay equity analysis must be run quarterly against AI-generated recommendations, not just annually at review time."
— Judgment (10%): the compensation professional and hiring manager review the recommendation against the full context of the candidate and the role.

Pay equity analysis has gotten more sophisticated. AI pay tool governance has not kept pace. The rules layer that would catch bias in the data before it reaches the offer letter is the gap.

[KEY TERMS]

Compa-Ratio: An employee's actual pay divided by the midpoint of their salary band. Used to assess pay positioning within a range. AI systems using compa-ratios can perpetuate historical pay disparities if the ratio reflects past underpayment.
Market Pricing Data: Survey data on what comparable roles pay in the external market. Used to set salary bands. If the underlying surveys reflect a market with historical pay disparities, AI tools trained on that data reproduce those disparities as 'market rate.'
Right to Explanation: A legal right — established under GDPR and emerging in US state laws — for individuals to receive a meaningful explanation of automated decisions that significantly affect them, including pay decisions.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Pay Tool Audit]

If your organization uses AI or algorithmic tools in any part of compensation decisions — offer generation, merit recommendations, equity refresh, pay band placement — answer these questions:

1. What data sources does the tool use, and have those sources been analyzed for historical bias?
2. Does the tool produce recommendations that differ by demographic group for employees in comparable roles? Has anyone checked?
3. Can a manager explain to an employee why they received a specific pay recommendation?

If AI recommends a pay move, what facts would you insist on seeing before approving it?
