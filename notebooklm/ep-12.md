The EU AI Act: 500 million people, a four-tier risk system, and what it means for HR

[When the Regulator Leads]

The United States has taken a largely voluntary approach to AI governance — guidelines, frameworks, recommendations.

The European Union took a different path.

In 2024, the EU passed the AI Act: the world's first comprehensive legal framework for regulating artificial intelligence. It applies to any organization deploying AI systems that affect people in the EU — including non-European companies. It carries fines of up to 7% of global annual revenue for the most serious violations.

For HR professionals: employment is explicitly named as a high-risk domain. That means the tools you're using — or might buy — are subject to the most demanding tier of requirements under the law.

[CASE FILE — REGULATION EU 2024/1689 — European Union — phased implementation 2024–2027]

The Four-Tier Risk System

The EU AI Act classifies every AI system into one of four risk tiers:

UNACCEPTABLE RISK (Banned): Systems that manipulate people against their will, exploit vulnerabilities, enable social scoring by governments, or conduct real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. These are prohibited outright.

HIGH RISK (Heavily Regulated): Systems used in areas where AI can significantly affect people's rights, safety, or livelihoods. HR and employment is explicitly listed in this category. Covered systems include: AI used in recruitment and candidate selection, AI used in promotion and performance evaluation, AI used in task allocation and monitoring of behavior.

LIMITED RISK (Transparency Required): Chatbots and AI that interacts with people must disclose that it's AI.

MINIMAL RISK (No Specific Requirements): Most AI, including spam filters and AI-generated playlists.

If your HR AI tool falls in the High Risk category — and most significant ones do — here's what you're required to do before deploying it: register it in an EU database, conduct a conformity assessment, maintain technical documentation, implement human oversight, ensure transparency to affected workers, and conduct ongoing monitoring.

"AI systems used in employment, workers management, and access to self-employment are among the most consequential AI applications and deserve strict requirements."
— EU AI Act, Recital 57 — the legislative reasoning behind the high-risk classification of employment AI

[THE HR IMPLICATION]
Under the EU AI Act, emotion recognition systems in the workplace are prohibited in most contexts. AI systems that evaluate worker performance, monitor behavior, or allocate tasks are high-risk. Employers must be able to explain to affected workers how the system works and what data it uses. Workers have rights to contest AI-based decisions. This applies to any company deploying these systems for EU-based workers — regardless of where the company is headquartered.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

The EU AI Act essentially codifies ICM's factory-first principle into law for high-risk systems. It says: you may not run the machine until the governance factory is built.

Applying 60/30/10 to what the EU AI Act requires for high-risk HR AI:

— Code (60%): the AI system's logic, training, and outputs. The law doesn't prohibit this layer — it requires it to be documented.
— Rules (30%): the EU AI Act is, at its core, a mandate to build the rules layer before deployment. Conformity assessment, technical documentation, human oversight specifications, worker transparency obligations, complaints procedures — these are all rules that organizations must write and implement before the system is used. The law exists because companies were deploying the code layer without ever building the rules layer.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): the high-risk system's output is a bounded signal, capped at 10% of the decision weight. Not a determination of the regulated outcome.
— Human operator (above the stack): the ongoing human oversight the law requires be meaningful and documented — not ceremonial — sits above the workflow, applies the rules, and owns the decision.

The EU AI Act's prohibition on emotion recognition in workplaces is an example of a rule that exists because no organization voluntarily wrote "we will not use unvalidated psychological inferences to evaluate employees." When organizations skip the rules layer, regulators eventually write it for them — at continental scale, with fines up to 7% of global revenue.

[KEY TERMS]

High-Risk AI System: Under the EU AI Act: AI systems used in safety-critical or rights-impacting contexts, including employment decisions. Subject to conformity assessments, documentation requirements, human oversight mandates, and transparency obligations.
Conformity Assessment: A required evaluation confirming that a high-risk AI system meets the EU AI Act's requirements before deployment. For most HR AI, this involves internal assessment with third-party review.
Prohibited AI Practices: Under the EU AI Act: AI systems that manipulate people using subliminal techniques, exploit vulnerabilities, enable government social scoring, or conduct real-time public facial recognition. Banned outright regardless of claimed benefit.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Risk Classification Exercise]

Think about three AI tools your organization uses that affect employees — anything from a hiring screener to a performance management tool to a scheduling algorithm.

For each one, decide: under the EU AI Act's framework, would it be Unacceptable Risk, High Risk, Limited Risk, or Minimal Risk?

Then ask: if these tools are High Risk, are the required protections in place — human oversight, technical documentation, worker transparency, and a complaints process?

Which of the EU requirements would your current tools fail today?
