[The Manager Who Reviewed 40 Files in 90 Minutes]

We introduced the concept of automation bias in Day 10: the human tendency to trust and defer to automated recommendations, especially when the system is presented as objective and the reviewer is busy.

Performance management is where this becomes most dangerous.

Consider a typical scenario: an AI performance management system has generated ratings for 40 employees on a manager's team. The manager opens each file. The AI has suggested ratings, drafted narrative feedback, and flagged three employees for performance improvement plans. The manager clicks through. Some files take 20 seconds. The complex ones take two minutes.

At the end of 90 minutes, 40 performance reviews have been 'completed.'

But were they? Or were they approved?

The difference matters enormously for governance — and for the employees whose careers those documents will follow.

[CASE FILE — OECD REPORT, 2023 — Worldwide — documented across multiple industries]

Algorithmic Management at Work

The OECD defines algorithmic management as 'the use of data-driven tools to monitor, evaluate, and direct workers in real time.'

In its 2023 report on algorithmic management in the workplace, the OECD documented the spread of these systems across industries — from gig platforms (which we covered in Day 8) to traditional office environments.

Key findings:

PERFORMANCE MONITORING: AI systems that track keystrokes, application usage, communication patterns, and work output have become standard in many knowledge work environments. The data feeds directly into performance evaluations.

FEEDBACK GENERATION: AI systems that draft performance review language based on logged data are increasingly common. In some organizations, managers primarily edit rather than author performance narratives.

PIP TRIGGERING: Systems that automatically flag employees for Performance Improvement Plans based on measured outputs — without requiring a manager to identify the pattern first.

THE ACCOUNTABILITY GAP: When AI flags an employee for a PIP and a manager approves it in a brief review, who made the decision? The employee may experience it as a manager's judgment. The manager may experience it as confirming what the data showed. Neither is fully accurate — and neither is fully accountable.

[THE GOVERNANCE RISK]
AI-generated performance assessments can encode the same biases as AI hiring tools — if the training data reflects historical patterns where certain groups were rated lower, the AI will rate them lower. Research has documented that AI feedback systems can replicate gender, race, and disability biases present in historical reviews. The difference from hiring: the employee is already inside the organization and may have no visibility into how the system assessed them.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

AI performance management is a case where the 60/30/10 principle is violated so consistently it's become the default.

Most organizations deploying AI performance tools have built the code layer and left the rules layer empty. The manager clicking through 40 reviews in 90 minutes is operating in a rules vacuum — there's no specification of how long a meaningful review should take, what they must look at beyond the AI output, or when they are obligated to override.

— Code (60%): collect performance data, generate ratings, draft feedback narratives. This is what the tool does.
— Rules (30%): the required rules that most organizations never write: "a manager review must take a minimum of X minutes per employee," "any AI-generated narrative used verbatim must be disclosed to the employee as AI-drafted," "if a manager approves more than 85% of AI recommendations without modification across a review cycle, that manager's reviews are automatically flagged for quality check," and "feedback for employees on leave, with accommodations, or with recent complaint history requires senior HR review before delivery."
— Judgment (10%): the manager's actual assessment of their employee — informed by data, not replaced by it.

Meaningful human oversight isn't created by putting a checkbox in front of a manager. It's created by designing a rules layer where the review conditions are specified before the system goes live.

[KEY TERMS]

Algorithmic Management: The use of AI systems to monitor, evaluate, direct, and discipline workers in real time — moving managerial functions from humans to software. Documented to increase work intensity and reduce worker autonomy.
Automation Bias: The tendency to over-trust automated recommendations and under-scrutinize them, especially under time pressure. A major risk in AI-assisted performance reviews where the AI recommendation shapes the manager's frame before they assess.
Performance Improvement Plan (PIP): A formal document placing an employee on notice that their performance is insufficient and specifying required improvements. AI-triggered PIPs raise due process and discrimination concerns when the triggering logic is opaque.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Review Quality Test]

Think about the last performance review cycle at your organization.

1. What role did AI or automated tools play in generating ratings, drafting feedback, or flagging employees?
2. What was the expected time for a manager to complete each review?
3. How often do managers override or significantly modify AI-generated content?

At what point does AI-supported performance management become algorithmic management? What is the line your organization has drawn — and is it written down anywhere?
