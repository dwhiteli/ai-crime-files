[Why We Need a Map]

You've spent three weeks learning about AI governance failures and the laws written in response to them.

Now we turn to the specific rooms where AI is making decisions about people at work.

The employee lifecycle is a useful frame: from the moment a person first encounters your company as a candidate, through onboarding, development, performance, compensation, and eventual departure, AI can be — and increasingly is — present at every stage.

Most HR leaders don't have a complete map of where AI is operating in their organization. Tools get purchased by different teams. Vendors bundle AI features into SaaS platforms without announcing them. What starts as a 'workflow automation' quietly becomes an AI-influenced decision.

The map is the first governance act. You can't govern what you haven't located.

[THE MAP: WHERE AI TOUCHES HR]
ATTRACT: Job posting optimization (which roles to advertise, where, to whom). Programmatic job advertising (algorithmic targeting of candidates — Facebook housing ad risk applies here). Candidate sourcing (AI-generated prospect lists from LinkedIn and other platforms).

HIRE: Resume screening and ranking. Chatbot pre-screening conversations. Video interview analysis (facial expression, voice tone, word choice). Assessment scoring. Background check flagging.

ONBOARD: Personalized onboarding content. Document processing. Benefits enrollment assistance.

DEVELOP: Learning recommendation engines. Internal mobility and job matching. Mentorship pairing algorithms.

MANAGE: Performance management AI (feedback drafting, rating calibration, productivity tracking). Schedule optimization. Workload distribution. Attendance and behavior monitoring.

REWARD: Compensation banding recommendations. Merit increase suggestions. Equity refresh algorithms. Benefits utilization analysis.

RETAIN: Flight risk scoring. Engagement sentiment analysis. Succession planning recommendations.

SEPARATE: Voluntary separation targeting models. Severance eligibility screening. Exit interview analysis.

[The Invisible AI]

Here's the governance challenge most HR teams don't see coming: much of the AI in the HR lifecycle isn't purchased as an AI product.

Your ATS now has an AI matching score built in — it came with the software update you approved last quarter. Your HRIS has a 'talent insights' module your vendor bundled in free. Your performance management platform added an AI feedback drafting tool in a recent release.

Most of the AI operating in HR today came in through the side door of SaaS product updates.

This means your AI inventory can't be built by asking HR what AI tools they bought. It requires auditing the tools you have for AI features you may not have consciously adopted.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

The map-building exercise is ICM's observe-before-you-build principle applied to your entire HR technology stack.

For each system in the lifecycle: what AI features are active, what decisions do they influence, who sees the outputs, and are those outputs advisory or does the system filter before a human ever looks?

Applying 60/30/10 to this discovery exercise:

— Code (60%): what are all the automated systems running? This is the inventory question.
— Rules (30%): for each system, what rules govern when a human must review, what decisions the AI may not make alone, and what constitutes a decommissioning trigger? If those rules don't exist or haven't been written, that's the governance gap.
— Judgment (10%): who is the named owner who can answer these questions, and who has authority to pause a system that fails its rules?

The goal isn't a vendor list. It's a decision map — for each touchpoint in the employee lifecycle, what is the AI doing, what rules constrain it, and what would happen if it were wrong?

Days 18 through 23 walk through the six highest-risk touchpoints. Today is the establishing shot. Tomorrow the investigation gets specific.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — Build Your First Map]

Using the lifecycle categories above (Attract, Hire, Onboard, Develop, Manage, Reward, Retain, Separate), identify at least one AI system active in your organization at each stage.

For each one:
- Is it explicitly AI, or a feature bundled into another tool?
- Who in HR knows it's there?
- Who is responsible for monitoring it?

Then: across attract, hire, onboard, develop, manage, reward, and exit, where do you think AI creates the biggest upside and the biggest governance risk in your organization?
