Case #07: The Face You Can't Appeal

Facial recognition, wrongful arrests, and the three men Detroit's algorithm got wrong

Listen time: 35 minutes


---

[January 9, 2020]

Robert Williams was a 42-year-old software developer. He lived in a suburb of Detroit with his wife and two young daughters.

On the afternoon of January 9, 2020, he was in his driveway when Detroit police pulled up. They arrested him in front of his family, handcuffed him, and put him in a squad car.

His daughters watched from the porch.

He had been identified as a shoplifting suspect by a facial recognition system. The system had compared a grainy surveillance video from a Shinola store to a database of driver's license photos and returned a match.

The match was Robert Williams.

The match was wrong.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2020-01 — Detroit, Michigan]

The Computer Said It Was You

Williams was brought to a Detroit police station and held overnight. The next day, a detective showed him two photographs side by side: the blurry surveillance image and his driver's license photo.

Williams picked up both photos, held them next to his face, and said: "I hope you don't think all Black men look alike."

The detective's response, according to Williams: "The computer says it's you."

Williams spent 30 hours in jail. He was released after his wife paid $1,000 bail. It took months for the charges to be fully dropped.

The ACLU took his case. Investigators found that the facial recognition system used by Detroit police had identified Williams based on a low-quality video image — a match that no trained human examiner would have made.

More troubling: the detective knew the match had come from facial recognition, which Detroit police policy described as a "lead" requiring additional verification — not as standalone evidence. But no additional verification had been done before the arrest.

"I'm not sure if my daughters will ever forget seeing their father arrested. That's something I have to live with. And I'm one of the lucky ones — the charges were dropped."
— Robert Williams, speaking to The New York Times, 2020

[The Science Behind the Problem]

Robert Williams wasn't a fluke.

In 2018, MIT researcher Joy Buolamwini published a landmark study with co-author Timnit Gebru. They tested three major commercial facial recognition systems from IBM, Microsoft, and Face++.

The results were damning.

On lighter-skinned male faces, the systems had error rates below 1%. On darker-skinned female faces, error rates reached as high as 34.7%. The systems were 34 times more likely to misidentify a dark-skinned woman than a light-skinned man.

Why? Training data. The systems had been trained predominantly on images of lighter-skinned faces. They had learned to recognize what they had seen most. People with darker skin — and especially darker-skinned women — had been underrepresented in the data.

The research had been published. The error rates were known. Law enforcement agencies deploying facial recognition had access to this information.

Detroit deployed the system anyway.

[THE FINDING]
Robert Williams was not the only Black man wrongfully arrested by a facial recognition match. Michael Oliver was arrested in Detroit in 2019 on a similar pattern: a low-quality image, an algorithmic match, no adequate human verification. Nijeer Parks was arrested in Woodbridge, New Jersey, after a facial recognition system at a different police department produced the same kind of error. The pattern crossed jurisdictions. All three men were Black.

[The Policy That Wasn't Followed]

Here's a detail that makes this case particularly striking from a governance perspective.

Detroit Police Department had a policy about facial recognition. The policy stated explicitly that a facial recognition result was a lead — not evidence — and that officers should not make arrests based solely on a facial recognition match.

The policy existed. It was on paper. It was, in any reasonable definition, a governance document.

But the policy didn't work. Robert Williams was still arrested.

This is the difference between having a governance policy and having a governance system. A policy sitting in a manual doesn't stop a detective from trusting "the computer says it's you." A governance system would have built the verification step into the workflow — so that making an arrest without it was structurally impossible, not just technically prohibited.

Writing down the rules is the beginning of governance. Making those rules impossible to skip is governance that actually works. Detroit wrote the rules and stopped there.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

Detroit had a policy that facial recognition results were a lead, not evidence. They had the words written down. What they didn't have was a 60/30/10 structure that made those words real.

— Code (60%): the facial recognition system ran, compared images, and returned a match. That part worked as designed.
— Rules (30%): Detroit's policy was the beginning of a rules layer, but it was never enforced by the workflow. The required rules — "no arrest may be authorized solely on a facial recognition match," "a trained examiner must independently review the image comparison," "at least one additional evidence source must be documented before arrest" — existed in spirit but not in the system's actual steps. They were optional guidelines, not structural requirements.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): the facial recognition match is a bounded lead, capped at 10% of the decision weight. A signal that points investigators somewhere, not evidence that authorizes an arrest.
— Human operator (above the stack): a senior officer reviews the complete evidence package, applies the rules layer, and owns the arrest decision.

The 30% rules layer is what turns a policy document into a governance system. Detroit wrote the policy. They didn't build the rules into the workflow so that skipping them was structurally impossible.

Robert Williams was arrested because a detective trusted "the computer says it's you" in the absence of a rules layer that would have required him not to. The factory-first principle of ICM demands that the verification structure exist before the system is used in the field — not as a memo in a manual, but as required steps in the process.

[KEY TERMS]

Facial Recognition: An AI system that identifies a person by comparing their face to a database of known images. Current systems have significantly higher error rates on darker-skinned and female faces.
Algorithmic Lead vs. Evidence: The important legal and procedural distinction between a facial recognition match (which should be a starting point for investigation) and actual evidence (which justifies an arrest). Detroit's policy made this distinction; the practice ignored it.
Disparate Error Rate: When an AI system is significantly less accurate for certain demographic groups. Facial recognition systems have documented disparate error rates by race and gender — a governance risk that must be assessed before deployment.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Policy vs. System Test]

Think about a governance policy in your organization — a rule that exists on paper about how something should be handled.

Ask:
1. Is following this policy required by the workflow, or optional based on judgment?
2. Is there any way to audit whether it was followed in a specific case?
3. What happens to someone who skips it — is there a consequence?

If the answer to any of these is "I'm not sure" — you have a policy, not a governance system.
