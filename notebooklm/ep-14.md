What Microsoft, Google, and IBM actually committed to — and how to tell real governance from branding

[The Principles Problem]

After the scandals broke — Amazon's resume screener, COMPAS, Facebook's housing ads — the tech industry responded with a wave of AI ethics announcements.

Google published its AI Principles. Microsoft published its Responsible AI Standard. IBM launched its AI Ethics commitments. Dozens of other companies followed.

These documents share certain characteristics: they are publicly available, they use words like fairness, transparency, accountability, and human oversight, and they are extremely difficult to hold anyone to.

In 2018, Google published its AI Principles and pledged not to develop AI for weapons or surveillance that violated human rights.

In 2024, Google quietly removed that pledge after winning a military contract.

This is not to say that corporate AI ethics efforts are meaningless. Some are genuine. The problem is that most people — including HR professionals buying AI tools — can't tell the difference between a governance program and a marketing document.

[CASE FILE — MICROSOFT RESPONSIBLE AI STANDARD v2 — Redmond, Washington — published 2022]

What Real Commitments Look Like

Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard is one of the most specific corporate AI ethics documents publicly available.

Unlike principles documents that state values, Microsoft's Standard states requirements — specific, testable obligations that teams must meet before shipping AI systems.

Examples of what it actually requires:

For fairness: teams must identify the demographic groups potentially affected by a system, assess potential harms, and document how they measured and mitigated disparate impact. The documentation must exist, not just the intention.

For human oversight: systems in high-risk domains must specify who is responsible for oversight, what data they have access to, how they can intervene, and under what conditions the system must be paused.

For explainability: affected individuals must be able to understand, in plain terms, how a decision affecting them was made — and have a mechanism to contest it.

This is what moves a principles document toward a governance document: specificity, testability, and accountability.

"We need to move from ethics principles to ethics practice. Having the right values isn't enough. You need the right processes, the right roles, and the right accountability structures."
— Stanford HAI, Walking the Walk of AI Ethics in Technology Companies

[THE EVALUATION FRAMEWORK]
When evaluating a vendor's AI ethics commitments, ask five questions: (1) Are specific requirements stated, or just values? (2) Is there a named person or team responsible for compliance? (3) Is there external verification, or only self-assessment? (4) Has the company's behavior ever contradicted its stated principles, and how did they respond? (5) Does the document govern what they sell to you, or only what they build internally?

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

ICM's 60/30/10 principle is a useful lens for reading corporate AI ethics commitments — specifically for separating real governance from branding.

A principles document lives entirely in the judgment layer (10%). It tells you what the company values. It doesn't tell you whether those values constrain any actual code or process.

A governance document adds the rules layer (30%). Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard is a real example: it specifies required documentation, named responsibilities, and checkpoints before deployment. When you read it, you can see the rules that govern the code.

A governance system adds the monitoring layer: is the code actually running the way the rules require? Is someone checking? Is there a consequence when the answer is no?

The ICM framework distinguishes these three levels. Most corporate AI ethics pages are at level one — a statement of what the company believes. A small number are at level two — specific rules that constrain what gets shipped. Almost none are verifiably at level three — because demonstrating that the rules are enforced continuously requires ongoing transparency that most companies haven't committed to.

When you evaluate a vendor's ethics commitments, ask: does this document live in the judgment layer, the rules layer, or the monitoring layer? Your governance exposure depends on the answer.

[KEY TERMS]

AI Principles: High-level statements of values an organization commits to in developing or deploying AI. Necessary but not sufficient for governance — without specific requirements, accountability, and enforcement, principles don't constrain behavior.
Responsible AI Standard: A more specific governance document that translates principles into testable requirements. Microsoft's version is a public example of what a mature standard looks like — with required documentation, specific roles, and checkpoints before deployment.
Ethics Washing: The practice of making public ethics commitments that are not backed by meaningful governance structures — using ethical language primarily for reputational purposes rather than operational change.
