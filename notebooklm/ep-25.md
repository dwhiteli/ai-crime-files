When your AI was built by someone else — procurement governance and third-party risk

[The Indemnification Clause]

One sentence in a vendor contract is worth reading more carefully than almost anything else.

It's usually buried in section 14. Or section 19. Wherever the lawyers put the indemnification and limitation of liability provisions.

It says something like: 'Vendor shall not be liable for any claims arising from Client's use of the Software in violation of applicable law.'

Translation: if you use this tool in a way that breaks the law — even if the tool was designed in a way that makes breaking the law likely — the liability is yours, not theirs.

Buying the tool doesn't transfer accountability.

That's the Vendor Problem.

[CASE FILE — CASE NO. 23-CV-00770 — U.S. District Court, Northern District of California, 2023]

Mobley v. Workday

Derek Mobley applied for jobs at more than 100 companies between 2019 and 2020. He was rejected from all of them — companies using Workday's AI hiring and screening tools.

Mobley, a Black man in his forties with a mood disorder, filed a class action lawsuit alleging that Workday's AI screening algorithms discriminated against him and others on the basis of race, age, and disability.

The central legal question was novel: can a software vendor who is not the employer be held liable under federal anti-discrimination laws?

Workday moved to dismiss, arguing they were a vendor — not an employer, not an employment agency — and therefore not covered by Title VII, the ADEA, or the ADA.

The court largely denied that motion. It found that if Workday's tools were a proximate cause of employment decisions — if their algorithms were materially influencing who got hired — the case could proceed against the vendor directly.

For HR professionals, the significance goes beyond the legal outcome: vendors are not a liability firewall. The employee filing the complaint doesn't care whether the bias came from your code or their code. The EEOC doesn't either.

The employer who signed the contract and used the tool is accountable for the outcomes the tool produces.

"Buying the tool doesn't transfer the accountability. The employer is still the employer. Signing a software contract doesn't give you permission to outsource your legal obligations."
— Employment law commentary on Mobley v. Workday

[THE SIX CONTRACT TERMS THAT MATTER]
When evaluating or renegotiating any AI vendor contract, these are the provisions that determine your governance position:

1. AUDIT RIGHTS — Can you require an independent bias audit using your own customer data, not pooled client data? Who pays? How often? What happens if the audit finds problems?

2. MODEL CHANGE NOTICE — Must the vendor notify you before material changes to the underlying model, scoring logic, or feature weights? How much notice? What counts as 'material'?

3. VALIDATION DOCUMENTATION — Are you entitled to the technical validation documentation for the model as applied to your specific use case? Or only the vendor's standard marketing materials?

4. DATA HANDLING — Who owns the data your candidates and employees generate through the tool? Can the vendor use your data to train their model? What are your obligations to those individuals if they do?

5. ADVERSE IMPACT REPORTING — Does the vendor provide adverse impact analysis as a contract deliverable, or only on request, or not at all?

6. EXIT RIGHTS — What happens to candidate and employee data when you end the contract? Can you retrieve it in a portable format? Is there a transition assistance obligation?

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

Vendor due diligence is ICM's observe-before-you-build principle applied to a procurement decision. You are trying to understand what the tool actually does — from evidence, not from marketing — before committing to deploying it on real candidates and employees.

Applying 60/30/10 to vendor governance:

— Code (60%): what is the tool actually doing? What data does it use? How does it produce its outputs? Can you get independent validation of its methodology?
— Rules (30%): what rules does the vendor have governing model changes, bias monitoring, and adverse impact reporting? And — critically — what rules does your contract establish for your rights to audit, receive notice, and exit? A contract without audit rights and model-change notice is a rules-layer gap that runs for the life of the agreement.
— Judgment (10%): your governance team evaluates the full picture and makes a deployment decision — with the authority to say not yet.

The vendor discount — 'sign now for 15% off' — is a pressure tactic designed to exploit urgency and prevent the observe-before-you-build discipline. A tool that can only be approved under time pressure probably won't survive scrutiny.

Procurement owns the contract. Governance owns the question: does this contract give us the rights we need to actually run the rules layer for this tool?

[KEY TERMS]

Proximate Cause (AI liability): The legal concept that an AI vendor's tool may be directly responsible for discriminatory outcomes if it materially influences employment decisions — even if the vendor is not the employer. The Mobley v. Workday case established this as a viable legal theory.
Adverse Impact Analysis: A statistical evaluation of whether a selection tool produces significantly different pass rates across demographic groups. The 4/5ths rule (80% rule) is the standard threshold: if a protected group passes at less than 80% of the highest-passing group's rate, adverse impact is indicated.
Vendor Governance Risk: The governance exposure created by relying on a third-party vendor for AI that influences employment decisions. Includes: inability to audit, lack of model change notice, pooled vs. customer-specific validation, and contractual terms that prevent independent review.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Vendor Governance Checklist]

Review one AI vendor contract you currently have — or ask your procurement or legal team for access to one.

Using the six terms from today's Finding, audit the contract:
1. Do you have audit rights using your own customer data?
2. Are you entitled to model change notice before updates?
3. Does the contract give you access to independent validation documentation?
4. What are the data handling provisions for candidate and employee data?
5. Is adverse impact reporting a contract deliverable?
6. What are your rights when the contract ends?

For every provision that doesn't exist or is ambiguous: what would you add in the next negotiation cycle?

The goal isn't to make the vendor your compliance officer. It's to make sure you have the information you need to make decisions about a tool that's making decisions about people.
