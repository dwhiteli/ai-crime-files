Flight risk scores, talent forecasts, and the governance questions predictive people analytics can't answer for you

[When the Algorithm Knows Before You Do]

A manager opens their HRBP dashboard on a Monday morning. Beside three of their direct reports' names is a small indicator: a flight risk score.

Sarah: High Risk. Marcus: Medium Risk. Aisha: High Risk.

The manager didn't ask for this information. The system generated it automatically from data the employees don't know is being analyzed — their engagement survey responses, internal mobility searches, email metadata patterns, time spent on internal job postings, frequency of 1:1 cancellations, and more.

What does the manager do with this information?

In organizations with good governance: they use it as a prompt to have a better career conversation. They look for context. They ask questions they might not have thought to ask.

In organizations with poor governance: they stop putting Sarah and Aisha on stretch assignments. They invest more in Marcus because he seems more stable. They make resource decisions based on predictions without telling the predicted-upon employees why.

The algorithm gave the manager a starting point. The manager's judgment — and the governance framework around it — determines whether it helps or harms.

[THE GOVERNANCE QUESTIONS]
For any predictive people analytics tool, governance requires answering these questions before deployment: (1) What data is the prediction based on, and is that data a valid predictor of the outcome being forecast? (2) Who sees the predictions, and what decisions are they authorized to make based on them? (3) Do employees know this analysis is occurring? (4) Is the model validated for disparate impact — does it predict risk equally accurately across demographic groups? (5) What happens when the prediction is wrong?

[The Compounding Risk]

Predictive people analytics has a compounding problem that other AI HR tools don't.

If a flight risk model flags Sarah as high-risk and her manager responds by giving her fewer development opportunities, Sarah becomes more likely to leave. The prediction creates the outcome it predicted.

This is called a self-fulfilling prophecy — and it's a known risk in predictive analytics generally. In HR, it has specific legal implications: if the model disproportionately flags employees of a particular demographic as flight risks, and those employees then receive fewer opportunities as a result, the model has created discriminatory outcomes through a mechanism that no one designed but everyone contributed to.

The fix is not to avoid predictions. It's to govern what happens after the prediction arrives — specifically, what decisions it can and cannot influence, and how those decisions are reviewed.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

Predictive people analytics is a domain where ICM's 60/30/10 principle has been almost universally violated in favor of the code layer alone.

— Code (60%): collect behavioral, performance, and engagement data; run the model; generate a flight risk score. Most organizations have built this layer thoughtfully.
— Rules (30%): this is the layer organizations consistently skip. The required rules — written before the dashboard goes live — should specify: who may see predictions, what decisions they may and may not influence, what disclosure must be made to employees, what the process is when a prediction appears to be wrong, and — critically — what the manager is explicitly prohibited from doing based on a prediction alone (such as removing an employee from development opportunities or succession planning). Without these rules, every manager invents their own policy.
— Judgment (10%): the human conversation — with the employee, about their actual situation. This is the part the model cannot replace and should not be allowed to preempt.

The self-fulfilling prophecy risk — where a flight risk prediction causes reduced investment that causes actual departure — is a direct result of operating without the rules layer. The rules layer is what prevents a prediction from becoming a self-fulfilling verdict. It must be built before the first manager sees their first dashboard.

[KEY TERMS]

Flight Risk Score: An AI-generated prediction of the likelihood that an employee will voluntarily leave the organization within a specified timeframe. Based on patterns in behavioral, performance, and engagement data.
Self-Fulfilling Prediction: When a predictive system's output changes how people are treated, which then causes the predicted outcome — creating a feedback loop the model was not designed to produce. A specific governance risk in flight risk and potential scoring.
Predictive Validity (HR analytics): The degree to which a predictive model accurately forecasts outcomes for the population it's applied to. Flight risk models must be validated for accuracy and for disparate impact across demographic groups before use in decisions.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Prediction Governance Framework]

If your organization uses any form of predictive people analytics — flight risk, high potential, attrition forecasting, anything — design the governance rules for that specific tool.

Answer:
1. Who can see the predictions?
2. What decisions is this output authorized to influence?
3. What decisions is it explicitly NOT authorized to influence?
4. How are employees informed that this analysis occurs?
5. What's the process when a prediction appears to be wrong?

When does predictive people analytics become helpful planning, and when does it become overconfident forecasting about people?
