Case #04: The Resume Killer

How Amazon built — and buried — an AI that taught itself to discriminate

Listen time: 35 minutes

[Previously, on AI Crime Files]

In Day 2, we used Amazon's resume screening AI as a quick example of how machine learning works — and how it fails.

Today we go back to the scene of the crime. Slower. With everything.

Because this case isn't just about bias in a hiring algorithm. It's about what happens when a powerful company builds a consequential system, watches it fail, tries to fix it, can't, and then quietly buries it — hoping nobody notices.

Somebody noticed.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2018-10 — Edinburgh, Scotland & Seattle, Washington]

The Edinburgh Project

In 2014, Amazon's machine learning team — based not in Seattle but in Edinburgh, Scotland — was handed an ambitious assignment.

Amazon received roughly 100 applications for every open role. Recruiters couldn't read them all. The Edinburgh team was asked to build something that could: an AI that would review resumes, learn what made a successful Amazon employee, and sort candidates automatically.

The system would be trained on ten years of resume data — specifically, the resumes of people Amazon had actually hired. It would identify patterns. Then it would apply those patterns to new applicants.

By 2015, the system was running. Recruiters fed it resumes. It spat out scores.

By 2016, something was wrong.

[What the Mirror Showed]

Researchers on the team began reviewing the system's recommendations. They noticed the algorithm was penalizing resumes that included the word "women's" — as in "captain of the women's chess team" or "women's engineering society president."

They dug deeper. The system had also downgraded graduates of two all-women's colleges.

It hadn't been programmed to do any of this. It had learned to.

Here's why: the training data — ten years of Amazon hiring decisions — was built in an era when the tech industry was overwhelmingly male. The algorithm had learned that male candidates got hired. So it learned to prefer male-coded resumes. It was doing exactly what it was designed to do: predict who Amazon would hire. The problem was that "who Amazon would hire" was itself a biased target.

The algorithm wasn't the source of the bias. It was a mirror. And the reflection was ugly.

"Amazon's computer models were trained to vet applicants by observing patterns in resumes submitted to the company over a 10-year period. Most came from men, a reflection of male dominance across the tech industry."
— Jeffrey Dastin, Reuters, October 2018

[The Failed Fix]

The team wasn't oblivious. When they found the bias, they tried to fix it.

They edited the model to make it gender-neutral — removing features that explicitly referenced gender. The bias persisted. The algorithm had learned subtler proxies: certain verbs, certain activities, certain school affiliations that correlated with gender without stating it.

They tried again. And again.

By 2017, Amazon's executives had concluded the problem couldn't be solved cleanly. The system was quietly disbanded. Recruiters were told it should not be used as the sole decision-making tool — which meant, functionally, it wasn't used at all.

In October 2018, Reuters broke the story. Amazon confirmed the system existed and had been shut down.

The system had operated for approximately three years.

[THE FINDING]
Amazon built a system to replicate its own hiring decisions, discovered its own hiring decisions were biased, tried to remove the bias from the system rather than from the decisions, and failed. The system ran for three years before being shut down. No public accounting of how many candidates it affected was ever released.

[The Questions That Were Never Asked]

After the Reuters story broke, executives and HR professionals around the world asked the obvious question: how did this get deployed without anyone checking for bias?

The answer is quieter than it sounds. The team did check. They found the problem. They tried to fix it.

The question that was never asked — before building the system — was this: "What if the historical data we're training on is itself biased? And if we can't remove that bias, do we stop?"

Nobody had defined in advance what would constitute a failure condition. Nobody had assigned ownership of the answer to that question. Nobody had built an off-ramp.

So when the bias was found, the only options were: keep trying to fix it, or quietly shut it down. There was no process for accountability. No mechanism to notify candidates who might have been affected. No external audit. No public disclosure until a journalist found it four years later.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

ICM's "observe before you build" principle asks teams to map what actually happens when a human makes this decision before they automate it. Amazon's Edinburgh team never did this.

If they had observed how their best human recruiters evaluated candidates, they would have found that recruiters were making judgments about potential, curiosity, and cultural fit — qualities that historical hiring data could not neutrally represent, because it was shaped by a decade of decisions that favored male candidates.

The 60/30/10 principle makes this concrete:

— Code (60%): screen for stated qualifications and requirements. Fully automatable.
— Rules (30%): a required rule: "if the training data has not been validated for demographic bias, the model may not make final ranking decisions." That rule did not exist before deployment. It should have.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): the AI's pattern-matching is one input, capped at 10% of the decision weight. It surfaces signal, not verdicts.
— Human operator (above the stack): a recruiter makes the final call on fit, applies the rules layer, and owns the hiring decision.

Amazon handed the entire decision to a system trained on biased history and called it objectivity. ICM's observe-before-you-build discipline would have surfaced the assumption — that historical Amazon hires were a fair training signal — before any code was written.

[KEY TERMS]

Proxy Discrimination: When an AI system discriminates based on a characteristic that's correlated with a protected class, even without directly using that characteristic. Amazon's algorithm penalized 'women's organizations' — a proxy for gender.
Training Data Bias: Systematic unfairness baked into an AI's historical training data. If the data reflects past discriminatory decisions, the AI learns to replicate those decisions.
Disparate Impact: When a seemingly neutral policy or tool produces significantly different outcomes across protected groups — even without discriminatory intent. A legal standard under Title VII.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Data Audit]

Think about one hiring or promotion decision your organization has made using historical data — even something as simple as "people who succeed in this role usually have X background."

Ask yourself:
1. Where did that belief come from? Whose decisions generated that history?
2. Were the people making those past decisions representative? Were the candidates?
3. If you built an AI on that history, what biases would it learn?

You just identified the most important question to ask before buying any AI hiring tool.
