Case #08: The Deactivation

How gig economy platforms terminate workers without human review

Listen time: 30 minutes


---

[The Email That Ends Your Job]

For millions of gig workers, the end of their working relationship with a platform arrives as an automated email.

No warning. No conversation. No manager. No hearing.

"Your account has been deactivated."

That's it. The algorithm made a decision. The algorithm sent the email. The algorithm — in many cases — is the only one who will talk to you about it.

For people who depend on these platforms for their income, that email is a firing. But the platforms don't call it that. They call it a "deactivation." And deactivations, they argue, aren't firings — because the workers weren't employees.

The legal debate about that distinction has been playing out in courts around the world. But the human reality is the same regardless of the legal label: a machine decided you were done, and there was nothing you could do about it.

[CASE FILE — CASE #2021-08 — Nationwide, United States]

The Amazon Flex Investigation

In 2021, Bloomberg Businessweek published an investigation into Amazon Flex — the program that allows independent contractors to deliver Amazon packages using their own cars.

Reporters found that Amazon had built an AI system to manage its Flex drivers — monitoring their performance, flagging violations, and terminating their contracts. The system tracked metrics like whether packages were delivered on time, whether the driver's app showed them as "online" during their shift, and whether customers complained.

When the system flagged a driver for enough violations, it generated an automated termination email. No human reviewed it. No manager approved it. The algorithm decided, and the email went out.

Drivers who received these emails and tried to appeal found a maze: chatbots, form submissions, weeks of silence. Some reported never receiving a response at all.

Among the drivers Bloomberg spoke to: a woman who had been deactivated because her GPS had shown her as briefly offline — she had driven through a tunnel. A man who was flagged for "quality" violations he couldn't identify. Both had strong performance records. Both were gone.

"Amazon essentially has drivers managed by an algorithm. The algorithm can fire you. The algorithm doesn't listen to appeals. The algorithm doesn't have a number you can call."
— Gig economy researcher, Bloomberg Businessweek investigation, 2021

[Uber and the Star Rating System]

Uber's deactivation system works differently but produces similar results.

Drivers must maintain an average rating above a threshold — typically 4.6 stars out of 5. Drop below it, and Uber will deactivate you.

The problem: passenger ratings are not objective. Research has documented that passengers rate drivers of color lower than white drivers for identical service. Drivers who work in lower-income areas, where passengers may be less likely to leave five-star ratings, face structural disadvantages.

A driver who is rated 4.5 stars by passengers who are systematically biased against them — through no fault of the driver — can be terminated by an algorithm that treats those biased ratings as objective performance data.

The algorithm doesn't know about the bias. It just calculates the average.

In 2022, the UK Supreme Court ruled that Uber drivers were "workers" — not independent contractors — entitled to minimum wage and other protections. Similar rulings followed in other European countries. In 2024, the EU Platform Work Directive required platforms to provide human review for automated deactivation decisions.

In the United States, the legal battle continues.

[THE FINDING]
Gig economy platforms have built the largest automated workforce management systems in human history — managing, monitoring, and terminating workers at a scale no human HR team could match. The efficiency gain is real. So is the risk: when the algorithm is wrong, the worker has no one to call.

[The Appeal That Goes Nowhere]

What happens when a gig worker believes their deactivation was wrong?

In most cases: very little.

Platforms have built appeals processes — but many of them are handled by AI too. You submit your appeal. It goes to a queue. An algorithm reviews it using the same data that triggered the original deactivation. The appeal is denied.

Drivers who have persisted — filing complaints with labor boards, reaching out to journalists, organizing with worker advocacy groups — have sometimes won reinstatement. But the process requires knowledge, time, and resources that many workers don't have.

This is not accidental. Platforms have calculated that the cost of a robust, human-staffed appeals process would be higher than the cost of occasionally losing a good driver to an erroneous deactivation. The algorithm is cheaper than the appeals coordinator.

That calculation is, at its core, a governance decision. Someone decided that human review of deactivations wasn't worth the cost. That decision has consequences that fall entirely on the workers.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

Gig platforms built their deactivation systems as if 60/30/10 didn't apply — as if the code was enough and nothing else was needed.

— Code (60%): detect GPS signal loss, calculate delivery time, flag customer complaints. This is what the platforms built, and they built it well.
— Rules (30%): this layer was almost entirely absent. Required rules would have included: "GPS anomalies in known dead zones (tunnels, underground parking) are automatically excluded from violation counts," "a worker with a strong 6-month record cannot be deactivated on a single data-point event without supervisory review," and "any deactivation must include a written summary of the specific data that triggered it." None of these rules existed. The code ran directly to the outcome.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): the platform's anomaly detection is a bounded signal, capped at 10% of the decision weight. A pattern that surfaces a case for review, not a deactivation trigger.
— Human operator (above the stack): a delivery manager examines flagged workers, applies the rules layer, and owns the deactivation decision.

The driver deactivated for driving through a tunnel, the driver flagged for quality violations he couldn't identify — both would have been protected by a functioning rules layer. The rules weren't written because nobody mapped the actual human workflow before automating it. A delivery manager who had ever fired a driver manually would have said immediately: "You can't fire someone for a tunnel."

Observe before you build. The EU Platform Work Directive eventually forced platforms to add the human review step the ICM principle would have required from day one.

[KEY TERMS]

Algorithmic Management: The use of AI systems to monitor, evaluate, direct, and discipline workers in real time. Common in gig economy platforms and increasingly in traditional workplaces.
Deactivation: The platform economy's term for terminating a worker's access to the platform. Legally distinct from employment termination in the US, but functionally identical in impact.
Surrogate Discrimination: When an AI uses a metric that seems neutral (like star ratings) but systematically disadvantages workers from certain groups due to bias in how that metric is generated.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — The Human Review Audit]

Identify one automated system in your organization that makes decisions that significantly affect employees — performance ratings, schedule assignments, workload distribution, anything consequential.

Ask:
1. Can this system produce an outcome that negatively affects an employee without a human reviewing it first?
2. If an employee believes the outcome is wrong, who do they talk to?
3. Is that person empowered to override the system?

If the answer to question 1 is yes and the answer to question 3 is uncertain — you have an algorithmic management problem.
