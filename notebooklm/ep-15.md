When the federal government didn't act, the states did — and now HR has a patchwork to navigate

[The Federal Silence]

As of 2025, the United States has no comprehensive federal AI law.

Congress has held hearings. Agencies have issued guidance. The White House published an Executive Order. But there is no single law that tells employers what they can and cannot do with AI in the workplace — the way the Civil Rights Act told them they couldn't discriminate, or HIPAA told them how to handle health data.

Into that silence, the states moved.

Today, if your company operates in multiple US states — and most medium-sized and large employers do — you may be subject to a patchwork of overlapping, sometimes conflicting AI employment laws. Knowing which ones apply to you is no longer optional.

The Laws You Need to Know

ILLINOIS — AI Video Interview Act (2020): The first state AI employment law. Requires employers using AI to analyze video interviews to notify candidates, explain how the AI works, get consent, and delete videos on request. Also requires annual race/ethnicity reporting if AI is the sole determinant for in-person interview selection.

NEW YORK CITY — Local Law 144 (effective 2023): Already covered in Day 11. The gold standard for bias audit requirements.

COLORADO — SB 205 (2024, effective 2026): Applies to deployers of 'high-risk' AI systems in employment decisions. Requires risk management programs, impact assessments, annual reviews, candidate notices, data correction rights, appeals path, and public disclosures. The first state to impose a 'reasonable care' duty of care specifically for AI bias.

CALIFORNIA — Two frameworks now apply. The Civil Rights Council regulations (2024) apply the state's anti-discrimination law to automated employment systems — same disparate impact standards as human decision-makers. Separately, CPRA Automated Decision-Making Technology rules took effect January 1, 2026: employees and candidates now have the right to opt out of automated decisions for hiring, discipline, or promotion; a pre-use notice explaining the logic behind the tool; and the right to request how their data was used.

MARYLAND: Prohibits facial recognition during interviews without written applicant consent, with civil penalties for violation.

NEW JERSEY (January 2025): The Division on Civil Rights issued guidance that the Law Against Discrimination applies to AI-driven bias. Employers cannot outsource their legal liability to AI vendors.

CONNECTICUT — SB 5 (passed April 2026): Requires detailed written notices for automated employment-related decision technology. A recent example of how fast the patchwork is expanding.

MASSACHUSETTS: The state's lie-detector prohibition (M.G.L. c. 149 § 19B) is broad enough that AI tools claiming to score honesty, integrity, or truthfulness may fall within its scope. AI behavioral assessments deployed in Massachusetts carry specific legal risk that does not exist in most other states.

As of April 2026, over a dozen states have enacted or are actively advancing AI employment legislation. This list will be longer by the time you read it.

[THE PATCHWORK PROBLEM]
Operating in multiple states means managing multiple compliance standards — some of which conflict. A tool that is compliant in one state may require additional documentation in another, candidate notices in a third, and specific audit results in a fourth. HR professionals need a state-by-state compliance map for every AI tool they deploy, updated as laws evolve. Most organizations don't have this.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

Multi-state AI compliance is an ICM problem at scale, and the 60/30/10 principle applies directly.

— Code (60%): building and maintaining the AI tools themselves. This is what most organizations focus on.
— Rules (30%): this is where multi-state compliance lives. Each state law is a set of rules — bias audit requirements, candidate notice obligations, appeal rights, data correction windows. The rules layer for a multi-state employer must capture every applicable requirement before a tool is deployed in that state. A tool deployed without this rules layer is running blind into legal exposure.
— AI judgment (10% ceiling): any AI-driven compliance signal — flagging probable bias exposure, surfacing a notice gap — is a bounded input, capped at 10% of the decision weight. Not the deployment authorization.
— Human operator (above the stack): a compliance owner reviews the checklist for edge cases, applies the rules layer, and owns the deployment decision.

The factory-first principle is clear here: before a tool goes live in a new state, the compliance factory for that state must be built — the rules reviewed, the notices drafted, the accommodation pathways confirmed. Connecticut's AI law passed in April 2026 — and Colorado's, California's, and several others have moved or expanded in the same window. That's how fast the rules layer is changing.

A policy document that says 'we comply with applicable state AI laws' is not a governance system. A workflow that checks each tool against each state's current requirements before deployment — and updates that workflow as laws change — is.

[KEY TERMS]

Regulatory Patchwork: The current US state of AI law — no federal standard, multiple state laws with different requirements, scope, and enforcement mechanisms. Requires employers to track and comply with requirements in every state where they operate.
High-Risk AI (Colorado): Under Colorado SB 205: AI systems that make or substantially influence consequential decisions in employment, including hiring, compensation, promotion, demotion, and termination. Triggers the law's full set of obligations for deployers.
Disparate Impact Analysis: Under California's Civil Rights Council regulations: employers using automated employment decision systems must analyze whether those systems produce selection rates that adversely affect protected groups — the same standard applied to human decision-making.
