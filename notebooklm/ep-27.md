Building an AI governance committee — roles, decision rights, and operating rhythm

[The Room Where It Happens]

Every governance program has a room — physical or virtual — where the people with decision authority over AI systems meet.

If that room doesn't exist, the decisions happen anyway. They happen in vendor sales calls. They happen in software procurement processes where the buyer is evaluating features, not risk. They happen when a manager clicks 'enable' on a new platform capability. They happen everywhere except where they should: in a structured, documented, cross-functional review.

Building an AI governance committee means designing that room.

Who sits in it. What authority they have. What evidence they require before a decision is made. How they document their choices. And crucially — who can say 'not yet,' and have that decision respected.

[CASE FILE — GOVERNANCE MODEL — Redmond, Washington — 2019 to Present]

Microsoft's Responsible AI Board

Microsoft is one of the few large organizations to publicly discuss the mechanics of its internal AI governance structure — not just its principles.

Their model: a cross-functional Aether Committee (AI and Ethics in Engineering and Research) supported by a dedicated Responsible AI Standards team. The committee has real decision-making authority — including the ability to halt product features — not just advisory or reporting functions.

The design principle they have been public about: the committee must have authority, not just visibility. A governance committee that can only observe or advise is a reporting function. A governance committee that can pause a deployment, require additional evidence, or escalate to executive leadership is a governance function.

For HR AI specifically, the implication is direct: whoever sits in the HR AI governance room needs to be able to say 'not yet' — and have that decision respected by the business unit, the HRIS team, and the vendor.

Without that authority, the room is theater.

"Governance without authority is theater. The committee needs to be able to stop things, not just document them. The hardest part of building an AI governance committee isn't designing it — it's getting leadership to give it real power."
— HR technology governance practitioner

[THE GOVERNANCE COMMITTEE — CORE MEMBERS]
A functional HR AI governance committee needs representation from seven domains:

1. LEGAL & EMPLOYMENT LAW — Regulatory exposure, new law monitoring, compliance with Title VII, ADA, ADEA, NYC LL144, Colorado SB 205, NLRA.

2. PRIVACY & DATA PROTECTION — What data is collected, how it's used, whether data subject rights are addressed, vendor data handling obligations.

3. HR OPERATIONS — The practical reality of how tools are being used versus how they were approved. Closest to actual deployment.

4. PEOPLE ANALYTICS — Technical documentation review, vendor claim evaluation, bias testing commission or execution.

5. INFORMATION SECURITY — Vendor security practices, data handling, access controls, incident response.

6. EMPLOYEE RELATIONS / LABOR RELATIONS — Employee experience signals, accommodation request management, collective bargaining implications in unionized contexts.

7. BUSINESS / LINE HR — The actual use case and business purpose the tool was acquired to serve. Governance needs to know what it's governing.

The committee also needs a defined mechanism for employee voice: how do affected employees surface concerns, request accommodations, or appeal AI-influenced decisions? Without this, the committee governs at a distance from the people it's protecting.

[INTERPRETABLE CONTEXT METHODOLOGY — COLD CASE FILE]

The governance committee is the institutionalization of all three ICM principles — observe before you build, 60/30/10, and factory first — as a permanent organizational practice.

Without the committee, these principles are applied inconsistently, if at all: a careful manager here, a thorough vendor review there, but no structural guarantee.

The committee makes the principles structural:

— Observe before you build: the committee's intake process requires that every new tool be mapped from multiple perspectives before approval — including the perspective of the employees it will affect, not just the business owner requesting it.
— 60/30/10: the intake standard requires documentation of what the code does, what rules govern it, and where human judgment is required. A tool with no defined rules layer does not receive approval.
— Factory first: the committee's authority — including the power to say "not yet" — is the governance factory. It must be built and functioning before consequential AI is deployed, not assembled in response to an EEOC charge.

Every case in Phase IV had the same structural failure: no committee existed with authority to pause the process and ask who hadn't been heard from yet. The committee is the structure that makes that authority real — and exercises it before harm accumulates.

[KEY TERMS]

AI Governance Committee: A standing cross-functional body responsible for reviewing, approving, monitoring, and decommissioning AI systems within an organization. Distinct from a task force (temporary) or advisory board (no authority) — it has actual decision-making power, including the authority to pause deployments.
Intake Process: The structured review a proposed AI tool must complete before organizational approval. Typically covers: use case documentation, risk classification, legal review, technical assessment, pilot scope, accommodation plan, monitoring cadence, and escalation criteria.
Decommissioning Trigger: A pre-defined condition under which an AI tool will be paused, narrowed, or retired. Examples: audit findings showing adverse impact above threshold, active EEOC charge or litigation, failure to produce required validation evidence, vendor acquisition, or material model change without notice.

[YOUR INVESTIGATION — Design the Room]

You're building SummitPeak Systems' HR AI governance committee from scratch — the fictional company you'll meet in full on Day 29.

Using what you've learned over Phase V, design the structure:
1. Who are the seven members — by function and role, not by name?
2. What authority does the committee have that a normal advisory group doesn't?
3. What must any new AI tool provide before the committee approves it for use?
4. How often does the committee meet, and what triggers an emergency session outside the regular cadence?
5. Who does the committee report to? Who monitors whether the committee itself is functioning?

This is a design exercise, not a research exercise. You have built the knowledge to answer these questions. Trust it.
